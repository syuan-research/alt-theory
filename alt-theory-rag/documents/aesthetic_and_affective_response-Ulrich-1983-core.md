===SECTION===
## Summary
This paper advances a psychoevolutionary framework to explain human aesthetic and affective responses to natural environments. It challenges the "cognitive primacy" perspective by arguing that affective reactions—such as liking or fear—often precede conscious cognition. These initial responses are triggered by "preferenda" (gross structural properties) and serve adaptive functions by motivating approach or avoidance behaviors essential for survival and well-being.

===SECTION===
## Concept: Affective Primacy
Affective response is the initial level of response to the environment, occurring quickly and often independently of conscious recognition or identification. 
---
Drawing on Zajonc's work, the framework posits that many affects are precognitive. These quick-onset responses structure subsequent cognitive processes, increasing information-processing efficiency and facilitating survival-related behaviors like fleeing from danger or staying in a beneficial setting.

===SECTION===
## Concept: Preferenda
Preferenda are gross, often vague, configural aspects or stimulus characteristics that are highly effective in eliciting affect despite being insufficient for detailed cognitive judgment.
---
In natural environments, preferenda typically include gross structural/configurational aspects, depth properties that require little inference, and general classes of environmental content (e.g., vegetation and water). These features allow an observer to grasp the "ambiance" of a setting nearly instantaneously.

===SECTION===
## Core Proposition: The Psychoevolutionary Model
Human response to a natural scene follows a specific sequence: An observer's initial affective/arousal state influences visual perception. This leads to an "Initial Affective Reaction" based on preferenda, which triggers primary arousal (Arousal 2).
---
This initial stage then influences "Cognition" (the appraisal of the scene's significance for well-being). The cognitive process refines the initial affect into a "Post-cognitive Affective State," which results in a "Motivation or Action Impulse" (Arousal 3), potentially leading to adaptive behavior or functioning.

===SECTION===
## Concept: Predictors of Landscape Preference
The framework identifies several visual properties as major determinants of aesthetic preference:
1. **Complexity**: The number of independent elements. Preference follows an inverted-U-shaped relationship with complexity.
2. **Focality**: The degree to which a scene contains a focal point or area that attracts attention.
---
3. **Depth/Spaciousness**: The presence of unambiguous depth cues. Lack of depth elicits dislike due to potential hidden dangers.
4. **Ground Surface Texture**: Smooth, homogeneous textures are preferred as they signal ease of movement.
5. **Deflected Vistas**: Curved sightlines (mystery) that promise additional information if the observer moves deeper into the area.

===SECTION===
## Theoretical Context: Adaptation and Evolution
The framework assumes humans are biologically adapted to natural as opposed to built content because of the long period of evolution in natural settings. 
---
Adaptive behavior encompasses both observable actions (exploration) and non-motor activity (psychophysiological restoration). Feelings of "like" motivate approach/staying behaviors to exploit resources, while "dislike" motivates avoidance to prevent risk.

===SECTION===
## Theoretical Relationship: Affect vs. Interest
Preference (like-dislike) and Interest (curiosity/attention) are treated as independent dimensions of emotion. 
---
High complexity natural scenes may elicit strong interest and sustained attention but only low levels of preference if they are disordered. Conversely, high-preference views (e.g., park-like settings) do not necessarily require strong interest or lengthy processing if they are easily grasped.
