## Summary
This paper presents a theoretical analysis of privacy as a dynamic, interpersonal boundary control process. Moving beyond definitions that equate privacy solely with seclusion or withdrawal, Altman proposes a regulatory model where individuals and groups seek an optimal level of social interaction through various behavioral mechanisms. The theory explores the dialectic nature of opening and closing the self to others and the critical role of privacy in defining self-identity.

===SECTION===
## Core Concept: Selective Control of Access
Privacy is defined as the "selective control of access to the self or to one's group." This definition shifts the focus from static exclusion to an active regulatory process.
---
This process involves:
1. Various social units (individual-individual, individual-group, group-group).
2. A bidirectional flow (controlling inputs from others to the self and outputs from the self to others).
3. An active and dynamic regulatory mechanism rather than a passive state.

===SECTION===
## Core Proposition: The Dialectic Nature of Privacy
Privacy is a shifting dialectic process involving a continuing tension between forces driving people to come together and forces driving them to move apart.
---
Boundary regulation is not just about "keeping people out"; it is also about "opening up" to others. Social interaction reflects a momentary ideal level of contact that ranges from total accessibility to complete solitude, depending on the situation and time.

===SECTION===
## Core Proposition: Nonmonotonic Nature and Optimality
The theory assumes a nonmonotonic relationship between social interaction and satisfaction, meaning there is a "desired" level of privacy that represents an optimum balance.
---
Departures from this optimum lead to two states of imbalance:
1. **Crowding/Intrusion**: When achieved privacy is less than desired (too much social input).
2. **Social Isolation/Loneliness**: When achieved privacy is greater than desired (too little social input).
"Ideal" privacy is a homeostatic state of equilibrium on a continuum of interaction.

===SECTION===
## Concept: Privacy as a Boundary Process
Altman uses the analogy of a cell membrane to describe privacy boundaries. Just as a membrane changes permeability to maintain the viability of a cell, the interpersonal boundary becomes more or less permeable based on environmental and internal states.
---
The boundary serves two simultaneous components: a "keep out" (exclusion) component and an "open up" (intake) component. Territoriality and personal space are viewed as specific mechanisms used to regulate these boundaries.

===SECTION===
## Core Proposition: Behavioral Mechanisms of Regulation
Desired privacy levels are implemented through a complex system of integrated behavioral mechanisms that can substitute for or amplify one another:
1. **Verbal/Paraverbal Behavior**: Content (what is said) and structure (how it is said).
2. **Nonverbal Behavior**: Body posture, eye contact, and gestures.
3. **Environmental Behavior**: Use of personal space, furniture arrangements, and territory.
4. **Cultural Norms**: Socially learned customs and rules that dictate interaction patterns.

===SECTION===
## Theoretical Context: Functions of Privacy
Privacy serves three fundamental purposes that contribute to human functioning:
1. **Interpersonal Regulation**: Managing social interaction and limited communication to achieve balance.
2. **Self-Other Interface**: Integrating experiences with others to formulate future plans and weighing alternative behaviors during periods of reflection.
3. **Self-Identity**: The ultimate goal of privacy regulation. By controlling the permeability of self-boundaries, an individual defines the limits of the self, fostering a sense of autonomy, individuality, and self-realization.

===SECTION===
## Theoretical Dynamics: Costs of Regulation
The process of matching achieved privacy to desired privacy involves systemic "costs" or "prices." 
---
These costs may be physical (energy expenditure), psychological (stress, tension, anxiety), or physiological (heightened adrenal functioning). Costs accrue when boundary control is not achieved or when achieving it requires excessive personal resources.
