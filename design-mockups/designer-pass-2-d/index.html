Can you summarize the main critiques of fully autonomous tutoring agents in qualitative education research?

- Lin 2023 — "Autonomy gradients for qualitative research assistants"

Three lines of critique recur across the recent qualitative-education literature. First, reviewers consistently argue that fully autonomous tutoring agents overstate their epistemic authority; Patel & Okafor (2025) frame this as a human-in-the-loop necessity, where a researcher must retain interpretive control over analytic claims.

Second, Mendoza (2024) documents over-reliance risks: novice coders defer to the agent's tags even when those tags conflict with their own close reading, which erodes the reflexive discipline qualitative work depends on.

Epistemic trust is not granted once and then fixed; it must be re-negotiated at each analytic turn. — Mendoza, 2024, p. 41

Third, Lin (2023) proposes an autonomy gradient rather than a binary autonomous/assisted split, which the field has begun to treat as the default framing:

autonomy_level = negotiate(researcher_claim, agent_claim, evidence)

Net synthesis: the field treats unmediated autonomy as a methodological liability, not a design goal.

Our reflexive-coding framework already separates agent-suggested codes from analyst-accepted codes; the critique above sharpens why that boundary must stay explicit.
