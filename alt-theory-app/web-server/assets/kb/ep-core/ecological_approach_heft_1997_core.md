## Summary
This framework applies James J. Gibson’s Ecological Approach to Perception to the field of Environment-Behavior (EB) studies. Heft argues that traditional psychological theories have hindered EB research by focusing on internal mental representations rather than the environment itself. The ecological approach offers a radical alternative by positing that perception is direct, active, and based on information available in the environment ("ecological optics") rather than constructed in the mind.

The central pillar of this framework is the concept of **Affordances**—the functional possibilities an environment offers an individual. By shifting the unit of analysis from physical metrics to functional affordances, Heft proposes a new way to describe environments that is both objective and psychologically meaningful. Furthermore, the framework reinterprets navigation not as the consultation of a mental map, but as the dynamic perception of transitions between vistas over time.

===SECTION===
## Theoretical Context: Critique of Representationalism
The ecological approach is best understood in contrast to the dominant "Representational Theories" of perception. Traditional theories (from Locke to Information Processing) assume perception begins with a static, two-dimensional retinal image (a "snapshot").
---
**The Problem**: Because the retinal image is poor (2D, static), these theories assume the mind must add meaning, depth, and continuity through "mental representations" or schemas. This creates a dualism where the environment is physical/meaningless and the mind is psychological/meaningful.
---
**The Ecological Alternative**: Gibson argues that we do not perceive retinal snapshots. We perceive the **Ambient Optic Array**—structured light that fills the medium (air) and surrounds the observer. This light carries rich information about the layout of surfaces. Meaning is not added by the mind; it is discovered in the environment.

===SECTION===
## Key Concept: Ecological Optics and Invariants
**Ecological Optics** analyzes the interaction between light and the environmental surface layout. As an individual moves, the structure of the light changing around them (the flow) reveals the stable properties of the world.
---
**Invariants**: These are the persisting features of the environment that remain constant despite changes in the observer's position.
*   *Mechanism:* Perception requires action (locomotion). We do not see "frozen" moments; we perceive over time. By moving through the environment, the changing perspectives reveal the invariant structure (e.g., the rigid shape of an object or the layout of a room).
*   *Proposition:* "What is invariant does not emerge unequivocally except with a flux." Static perception is a limiting case; active exploration is the norm.

===SECTION===
## Core Concept: Affordances
**Definition**: "The affordances of the environment are what it offers the animal, what it provides or furnishes, either for good or ill" (Gibson, 1979). They are the functionally significant properties of the environment.
---
**Relational Nature**: Affordances are neither purely objective (physical properties) nor purely subjective (mental needs). They are **relational**.
*   *Example:* A surface affords "sitting-on" (is a seat) only if it is approximately knee-height *relative* to the specific individual. A ledge that is a seat for an adult might be a "climb-on-able" feature for a toddler.
*   *Transaction:* This concept dissolves the dualism between person and environment. They are a system. The environment implies a certain kind of animal (a user), and the animal implies a certain niche.

===SECTION===
## Theoretical Application: Environmental Description
A major challenge in EB studies is describing the environment. Traditional methods use either **Physical/Structural** language (geometry, physics) which lacks psychological meaning, or **Subjective** language (feelings, semiotics) which detaches from the physical reality.
---
**Functional Taxonomy**: Heft proposes describing environments via affordances. This method is:
1.  **Objective**: Based on real physical properties (height, rigidity).
2.  **Meaningful**: Describes what can *be done* in the setting.
3.  **Inclusive**: Applies to both natural and built environments.

**Empirical Evidence: Children’s Outdoor Environments**
Heft re-analyzed Barker and Wright’s (1951) "One Boy’s Day" data to test this taxonomy. Instead of listing objects (tree, bench, fence), he categorized them by function.
*   *Result:* Disparate objects (bench, old crate, low tree limb) all grouped under the affordance "Climb-up-on-able." Conversely, a single object type (tree) could be split into multiple affordances (climbing, shading, hiding).
*   *Validation:* In a study of 25 children, a functional taxonomy based on affordances successfully predicted over 70% of the actual features children utilized in their play. This suggests that children perceive their environment primarily in terms of functional possibilities.

## Core Proposition: Navigation and Way-Finding
Traditional theories assume that because we cannot see a destination "here and now," we must use a **Cognitive Map** (a mental representation) to navigate. Heft argues this "spatializes" a temporal process.
---
**Navigation as Temporal Transition**:
From an ecological perspective, we perceive the structure of a building or city over time, not via a mental bird's-eye view map.
1.  **Vista**: What is seen from here (unhidden surfaces).
2.  **Transition**: The change occurring at an occluding edge (a corner, a doorway) where a new vista is revealed.
3.  **Looking Ahead**: Transitions afford "looking ahead." Navigation is the linking of successive vistas via transitions.

**Empirical Evidence: The Slide Presentation Study**
Heft (1983) tested whether "transitions" were the key informational unit for navigation.
*   *Method:* Participants viewed slides of a walk. Some saw the full route, others saw only the transitions between vistas.
*   *Result:* Participants exposed only to the sequence of transitions performed comparably to those who saw the whole route. When the temporal continuity of transitions was disrupted, performance degraded.
*   *Implication:* Way-finding relies on the detection of invariant structure revealed over time (transitions), not necessarily on constructing a static mental map.

===SECTION===
## Theoretical Distinction: Perception vs. Cognition
The ecological approach redefines the boundary between perception and cognition.
---
**Perception**: Awareness of *existing* things (objects, places, events) via the pickup of environmental information. It is not limited to the "present moment" but extends over time as the observer acts.
**Cognition**: Awareness of *non-existing* things.
*   *Remembering:* Awareness of things that no longer exist.
*   *Expecting:* Awareness of things that do not yet exist.
*   *Imagining:* Awareness of things that could happen.

**Conclusion**: This distinction removes the need for "mental maps" to explain simple perception of the environment. If a feature (like a building layout) can be revealed by moving through it, it is *perceived*, even if the whole building isn't visible at one instant.
