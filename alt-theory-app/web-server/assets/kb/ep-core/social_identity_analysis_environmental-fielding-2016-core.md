## Summary
This comprehensive review applies the **Social Identity Approach**—encompassing both **Social Identity Theory** (Tajfel & Turner) and **Self-Categorization Theory** (Turner)—to the domain of environmental attitudes and climate change. The authors argue that environmental challenges are fundamentally marked by an intergroup dimension, where conflicting social identities (e.g., political conservatives vs. progressives, farmers vs. scientists) drive polarization and inaction.

The central thesis is that an individual's environmental beliefs and behaviors are not merely the result of rational information processing or personal values, but are profoundly influenced by group memberships. When a specific social identity is salient, individuals assimilate their attitudes to the norms of that group to maintain a positive and distinct self-concept. The paper analyzes how ingroup norms, intergroup conflict, and the fluidity of identity shape environmental outcomes. Furthermore, it integrates the social identity perspective with established frameworks like **Cultural Cognition Theory**, the **Theory of Planned Behavior**, and **Value-Belief-Norm Theory**, arguing that social identity offers a dynamic, context-dependent lens that complements these static or individualistic models. Finally, the authors propose identity-based strategies for intervention, such as the use of ingroup messengers and the forging of superordinate identities.

## Core Theoretical Framework: The Social Identity Approach
The Social Identity Approach combines two interrelated theories to explain the relationship between the self and the social group: **Social Identity Theory** and **Self-Categorization Theory**.
---
**Social Identity Theory (Tajfel & Turner, 1979)**: This theory posits that a person's self-concept is comprised of both personal identity (idiosyncratic traits) and social identity (derived from group memberships). Individuals are psychologically motivated to maintain a positive and distinct self-concept. Consequently, they engage in intergroup comparisons that favor the ingroup over the outgroup, leading to ethnocentrism and ingroup bias.
---
**Self-Categorization Theory (Turner et al., 1987)**: This theory focuses on the cognitive processes of categorization. When an individual categorizes themselves as a member of a group, an accentuation process occurs: similarities within the ingroup are emphasized, and differences between the ingroup and outgroup are exaggerated. This leads to **depersonalization**, where the individual perceives themselves less as a unique personality and more as an interchangeable exemplar of the group, assimilating their thoughts and behaviors to the group prototype.

## Key Concept: Ingroup Norms and Assimilation
Within this framework, behavior is guided by **social norms**—the unwritten rules of how to behave as a group member. The influence of norms is contingent on the salience of the specific social identity in a given context.
---
**Injunctive vs. Descriptive Norms (Cialdini et al., 1990)**: The authors incorporate **Norm Focus Theory** to distinguish between *injunctive norms* (what is approved or desired by the group) and *descriptive norms* (what the majority of group members actually do). The social identity analysis suggests that individuals conform to these norms not just to avoid social sanctions, but to validate their status as "good" group members.
---
**Norm Alignment and Conflict**: When injunctive and descriptive norms align, the behavioral influence is strongest. However, conflict between norms (e.g., a group approves of energy conservation but wastes energy in practice) can undermine pro-environmental intentions. Furthermore, norms are most influential when they emanate from the behaviorally relevant reference group (e.g., "past guests of this hotel room" vs. "citizens in general").
---
**Identification Strength (Ellemers et al., 1999)**: The impact of ingroup norms is moderated by the strength of identification. Those who strongly identify with a group (e.g., "organic consumers" or "environmentalists") are significantly more likely to adhere to group norms regarding sustainable behavior than weak identifiers.

## Key Concept: Intergroup Conflict and Emergent Identities
Environmental issues often involve contested resources or ideologies, creating "David and Goliath" dynamics or ideological faultlines.
---
**Illegitimacy and Status**: Conflict arises when status differences between groups are perceived as illegitimate (e.g., rural landholders feeling oppressed by urban regulators). In these contexts, social identities become rigid, leading to stereotyping and moral exclusion (Opotow & Brook). Outgroups are denigrated (e.g., environmentalists labeled as "militant"), and ingroup members resist regulations perceived as imposed by an unfair outgroup.
---
**Opinion-Based Groups (McGarty et al., 2009)**: The authors highlight that environmental conflict can generate *new* social identities based on shared opinions rather than demographics. For example, "Climate Believers" vs. "Climate Skeptics" can function as distinct psychological groups (Bliuc et al., 2015). These emergent identities create **identity faultlines**, where objective appraisals of scientific evidence become psychologically impossible because accepting "outgroup" facts is seen as a betrayal of the ingroup.

## Key Concept: The Fluidity of Social Identity
Contrary to static personality traits, social identities are dynamic and context-dependent. The specific identity that guides environmental behavior changes based on the comparative context.
---
**Comparative and Normative Fit (Oakes et al., 1991)**:
*   **Comparative Fit**: The degree to which a social identity reflects real-world differences between groups in a specific setting.
*   **Normative Fit**: The extent to which the observed behavior matches the stereotypical expectations of the group.
---
**Dynamic Stereotyping**: The content of a group identity (e.g., "Are we pro-environmental?") is not fixed. It shifts based on the comparison target. For instance, a national group may view themselves as environmentally responsible when compared to a high-polluting nation, but less so when compared to a leader in sustainability. This shifting self-definition directly impacts the willingness to engage in sustainable behaviors.

## Theoretical Relationship: Cultural Cognition Theory
The authors compare the Social Identity Approach with **Cultural Cognition Theory** (Kahan; Douglas & Wildavsky), which argues that cultural worldviews (Individualistic-Hierarchical vs. Communitarian-Egalitarian) act as filters for risk perception.
---
**Integration**: Both theories agree that people filter environmental information through meaning-making structures. However, Cultural Cognition focuses on relatively static worldviews, while Social Identity emphasizes context-dependent group memberships.
---
**Proposition**: The authors propose that social identity may mediate the relationship between cultural worldviews and environmental attitudes. Individuals with individualistic values are likely to identify with conservative political parties; once that identity is salient, the specific norms of the party guide the response to climate change. Conversely, in contexts where a different identity is salient (e.g., a workplace with strong green norms), the influence of the static worldview may be attenuated.

## Theoretical Relationship: Theory of Planned Behavior (TPB)
The review integrates Social Identity with Ajzen's **Theory of Planned Behavior**.
---
**Addressing Weaknesses in TPB**: Subjective norms are often the weakest predictor in the TPB model. Social Identity Theory explains this by distinguishing between generic social pressure and specific **referent group norms** (Terry & Hogg).
---
**Proposition**: It is not the expectations of "important others" in general that predict intentions, but the norms of the specific, psychologically salient group. Furthermore, intergroup perceptions (e.g., urban vs. rural conflict) can serve as additional predictors of behavioral intention beyond the standard TPB variables.

===SECTION===
## Theoretical Relationship: Value-Belief-Norm Theory (VBN)
The authors address the relationship between Social Identity and Stern's **Value-Belief-Norm Theory**, which centers on personal norms and values.
---
**Integration**: The concepts are compatible because the self-concept includes both personal and social identities. Personal norms (feelings of moral obligation) are often derived from internalized social norms (Schwartz).
---
**Feedback Loop**: The authors propose a feedback loop where social identity influences personal norms. Being a member of a group that values the environment leads to the internalization of those values into personal norms. Conversely, individuals with strong personal environmental values are likely to self-select into groups that reinforce those values.

## Core Proposition: Social Identity Strategies for Intervention
The review moves beyond analysis to propose identity-based solutions for environmental problems.

**1. Use Ingroup Messengers**: Information is accepted based on trust and credibility, which are functions of shared identity. Messages regarding climate science or policy are more likely to be accepted if delivered by a member of the ingroup (e.g., a conservative politician speaking to conservatives) rather than an outgroup expert.

**2. Forge Superordinate Identities**: Conflict can be reduced by recategorizing distinct subgroups (e.g., farmers and environmentalists) into a single, inclusive superordinate group (e.g., "Protectors of the Region").
*   **The Common Ingroup Identity Model (Gaertner & Dovidio)**: This shifts the cognitive framework from "us vs. them" to "we," extending ingroup favoritism to former outgroup members.
*   **The ASPIRe Model (Haslam)**: Successful negotiation requires acknowledging subgroup identities first, then integrating them into a superordinate framework. Retaining subgroup distinctiveness (dual identity) is often crucial to prevent threat and ensure the legitimacy of the superordinate group.

**3. Social Identity Framing**: Leaders can shape the content of the group identity to be pro-environmental. This involves using inclusive language ("we," "us") to position renewable energy or conservation as congruent with the group's core values and history (Seyranian).

**4. Managing Norm Conflict**: When descriptive norms are negative (most people pollute), interventions should emphasize positive **injunctive norms** (most people *approve* of protection). Alternatively, communicators can make salient a higher-order identity that possesses positive norms (e.g., shifting focus from a "young adult" identity to a "citizen" identity).
