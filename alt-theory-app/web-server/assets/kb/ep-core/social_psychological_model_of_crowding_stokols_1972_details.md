## Empirical Study: Animal Populations (Calhoun & Christian)
The paper reviews foundational animal studies to establish the link between density and pathology.
---
**The Behavioral Sink (Calhoun, 1962)**: Observations of Norwegian rats in limited space showed "pathological behaviors" including neglect of maternal duties, hyper- and homosexuality, and social withdrawal.
---
**Deer Population (Christian et al., 1960)**: A herd of deer confined to a small island showed a pronounced decrease in reproductivity as population density increased, regardless of food supply.

===SECTION===
## Empirical Study: Human Census Data and Correlational Surveys
[Context: Urban Planning]
Review of studies utilizing census tract data to correlate density with social indices.
---
**Schmitt (1957, 1966)**: Correlated measures like persons per net acre with crime, tuberculosis, and suicide. 
---
**Mediating Factors**: Winsborough (1965) found that the correlation between density and pathology disappears when social status is controlled. Schmitt (1963) noted that in Hong Kong, cultural traditions and land use patterns mediate the effects of high density.

## Specific Example: The Jury Room
[Scenario: Physical Confinement]
The jury room serves as an example where behavioral withdrawal is impossible or costly.
---
Jurors in a cramped chamber cannot leave until a verdict is reached. To alleviate the stress of crowding, they may employ **perceptual or cognitive modes** of resolution. 
---
**Adjustment**: They may become "thoroughly engrossed in deliberation," effectively minimizing the salience of their physical surroundings by focusing on the gravity of the task.

===SECTION===
## Specific Example: The Football Stadium and The Astronaut
[Scenario: Task Absorption vs. Physical Restriction]
---
**The Football Stadium**: Thousands of fans are packed together. During the game, task absorption (watching the action) makes the spatial limitation go unnoticed. Once the game ends, the need for behavioral coordination (exiting) makes the spatial restriction immediately salient and stressful.
---
**The Astronaut**: An example of **nonsocial crowding**. An astronaut may feel crowded purely due to the cramped quarters of a space capsule, independent of the presence of others.

===SECTION===
## Empirical Study: Laboratory Investigations (Freedman et al.)
[Context: Human Task Performance]
The paper contrasts animal data with direct human laboratory experiments.
---
**Freedman (1970, 1971)**: Experiments demonstrated that when group size is kept constant but room size is varied (and physical discomforts like temperature are controlled), high density has virtually no ill effects on human task performance.
---
**Gender Differences**: Freedman observed that interpersonal relations within female groups were more friendly in small rooms, while male groups manifested more aggressive/affective behaviors.

## Raw Text Snippet: Definition of Crowding Stress
"A state of crowding exists, and is perceived as such by an individual, when the individual’s demand for space exceeds the available supply of such space... Recognition of spatial inadequacy arouses the experience of psychological and physiological stress." (p. 75)

===SECTION===
## Raw Text Snippet: Physiological Effects
"Physiological tests have revealed that crowding commonly results in an increased secretion of various hormones which affect the whole human physiology... crowding increases the risk of disease by heightening social and emotional strain, rather than by increasing the opportunity for spread of infection." (p. 81)
