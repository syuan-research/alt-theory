## Specific Example: The Blindfold Demonstration
[Context: Active Perception]
To demonstrate that action is integral to perception, the author describes a common phenomenon: If a familiar object is placed in the hand of a blindfolded person who is instructed to keep their hand perfectly stationary, identification is difficult.
---
**Result**: As soon as the person is allowed to manipulate the object (active touch)—turning it and exploring its surfaces—identification becomes easy. This illustrates that motor activity is not separate from perception but is a necessary component of it.

## Concept Illustration: The Ledge
[Context: Relational Affordances]
The author uses a 150 mm (6-inch) ledge in a public area to illustrate the relational nature of affordances.
---
**For an Adult**: The ledge affords functioning as an edge marker or a tripping hazard. It is generally not seen as a seat because the scale is wrong for an adult's body.
**For a Child**: The same physical ledge affords sitting, balancing, climbing, or jumping. The meaning of the object changes based on the body scale and action capabilities of the user.

## Empirical Study: Climbing and Body Ratios (Warren, 1984)
[Context: Biomechanics/Perception]
The author cites seminal work by Warren regarding the affordance of "climbability" for stairs.
---
**Findings**: A horizontal surface is perceived as "step-up-on-able" based on a specific ratio between the step height and the perceiver's leg length. While leg lengths vary between adults and children, the *ratio* (the Pi number) that marks the limit of climbability remains constant. This proves affordances are specifiable, quantifiable, and rooted in biological reality.

## Functional Taxonomy: Children's Outdoor Environments
[Context: Environmental Analysis]
Heft re-analyzed observational data from Barker & Wright (1951) to create a taxonomy of environmental features based on function rather than form. This list categorizes features by what children *do* with them:

1.  **Flat, smooth surface**: Affords walking, running, cycling, skating.
2.  **Relatively smooth slope**: Affords coasting, rolling, sliding.
3.  **Graspable/detached object**: Affords throwing, hammering, digging, building.
4.  **Attached object**: Affords sitting-on, jumping-over.
5.  **Non-rigid attached object**: Affords swinging-on.
6.  **Climbable feature**: Affords mastery, looking out from, passage to other levels.
7.  **Aperture**: Affords locomoting through, peeking/listening into adjacent places.
8.  **Shelter**: Affords microclimate, prospect/refuge, privacy.
9.  **Moldable material (dirt/sand)**: Affords construction, pouring, sculpting.
10. **Water**: Affords splashing, swimming, fishing, mixing.

## Raw Text Snippet: Sontag on Photography
"Photographic images tend to subtract feeling from something we experience firsthand and the feelings they do arouse are, largely, not those we have in real life." (Sontag, 1977: 68)
---
The author uses this to argue that photographic surrogates used in research create a "distance" that fundamentally alters the aesthetic experience compared to actual immersion in the landscape.
