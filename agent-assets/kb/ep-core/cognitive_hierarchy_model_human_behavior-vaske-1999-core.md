## Summary
This paper tests the **Cognitive Hierarchy Model** of human behavior within the context of natural resource management, specifically wildland preservation. The authors propose that general values regarding the environment influence specific attitudes, which in turn drive behavioral intentions (specifically, voting behavior). They empirically demonstrate that a single "Biocentric/Anthropocentric" value orientation continuum exists and that attitudes fully mediate the relationship between these value orientations and behavioral intentions. This supports the theoretical "inverted pyramid" structure where broad, stable values act as the foundation for more specific and changeable attitudes and behaviors.

===SECTION===
## Concept: Cognitive Hierarchy Model
The Cognitive Hierarchy Model suggests that human thoughts and actions are organized into a structure resembling an inverted pyramid.
---
*   **Values**: At the base are values—enduring, abstract beliefs that transcend specific situations (e.g., honesty, freedom). These are few in number, central to the belief system, and slow to change.
*   **Value Orientations (Basic Belief Patterns)**: These are patterns of basic beliefs that strengthen and give meaning to fundamental values regarding specific topics (e.g., wildland preservation).
*   **Attitudes/Norms**: These are more specific evaluations of objects or situations. They are more numerous than values.
*   **Behavioral Intentions/Behaviors**: At the top are specific actions or intentions to act. These are numerous, faster to change, and specific to situations.

===SECTION===
## Concept: Anthropocentric vs. Biocentric Value Orientations
The authors conceptualize environmental values along a single continuum rather than as mutually exclusive categories.
---
*   **Anthropocentric Orientation**: A human-centered view where nature’s primary value is instrumental—providing commodities (timber, minerals) or benefits (recreation) for humans. Nature is seen as material to be used.
*   **Biocentric Orientation**: A nature-centered view where ecosystems and species have inherent worth independent of their usefulness to humans. Human needs are important but viewed within a larger perspective where nature has a right to exist.

===SECTION===
## Core Proposition: The Mediation Hypothesis
A central proposition of the model is the causal flow: **Values $\rightarrow$ Attitudes $\rightarrow$ Behaviors**.
---
The authors hypothesize that value orientations do not directly predict specific behaviors (like voting). Instead, value orientations influence *attitudes* toward the specific object (e.g., wildland preservation), and these attitudes then directly influence the *behavioral intention*. This is known as "full mediation." If values influenced behavior directly without the attitude step, it would be "partial mediation" or a "direct effects" model.

===SECTION===
## Theoretical Context: Theory of Reasoned Action
The study integrates the **Theory of Reasoned Action** (Ajzen & Fishbein) into the hierarchy. This theory posits that most social behaviors are volitional and that the most direct predictor of behavior is the *intention* to perform that behavior.
---
Therefore, analyzing the determinants of "voting intention" is theoretically equivalent to analyzing the determinants of the voting behavior itself.

===SECTION===
## Theoretical Relationship: Wildlife Protection/Use
The Biocentric/Anthropocentric continuum for forests is theoretically linked to the "Protection/Use" continuum found in wildlife management literature.
---
Previous research (Fulton et al., 1996) identified wildlife belief dimensions that arrayed along a protection/use continuum. The authors argue these are conceptually similar: "Biocentric" aligns with "Protection" (inherent rights of nature), and "Anthropocentric" aligns with "Use" (management for human benefit).
