## Summary: The Configurational Theory of Architecture
Space Syntax, as articulated in "Space is the Machine," is a configurational theory of architecture that posits space is not a neutral background to human activity but a functional "machine" in its own right. The theory shifts the focus from the physical properties of individual buildings to the "spatial configuration"—the complex system of relations between spaces that accounts for other relations within the system. 
---
The theoretical preliminaries establish that architecture emerges when the "non-discursive" (unconscious) rules of building are raised to the level of conscious, reflective thought. By analyzing space as a system of topological and geometric relations, the theory provides a rigorous, analytic framework for understanding how the built environment generates patterns of movement, encounter, and social meaning.

===SECTION===
## Concept: Spatial Configuration
Configuration is formally defined as "relations which take account of other relations in a complex." It is distinct from simple "relation," which might only describe the adjacency or permeability between two elements (e.g., Space A is next to Space B). 
---
In a configurational system, the relationship between Space A and Space B is redefined by how each relates to a third space, Space C. This interdependence means that a change in one part of the system (like closing a door or moving a wall) can have global effects on the integration and accessibility of the entire complex. Configuration is the primary means through which social and cultural patterns are embedded in the material objectivity of buildings.

===SECTION===
## Concept: Non-Discursivity (Ideas to Think With vs. Ideas to Think Of)
Hillier introduces a fundamental distinction between two types of knowledge: "social knowledge" and "analytic knowledge." Social knowledge consists of "ideas we think with"—the unconscious, configurational rules that govern language, social behavior, and the use of space. 
---
In vernacular building traditions, these rules are handled like the grammar of a language; they are used automatically to reproduce culturally sanctioned patterns. Because these rules are used intuitively, humans find it difficult to talk about them or analyze them rationally. This is the "non-discursive" dimension of configuration. Architecture begins when these "ideas we think with" are turned into "ideas we think of"—becoming objects of conscious, speculative inquiry.

## Core Proposition: The Need for Analytic Theory
Because architecture moves away from the "safe" confines of cultural programming, it requires a theory to support the creative act. Hillier argues that most architectural theories have been "strongly normative" (telling designers what to do) but "weakly analytic" (failing to predict how designs will actually function). 
---
A truly analytic theory must be based on the direct study of buildings as non-discursive objects. It should provide an "abstract machine" that models the invariant processes responsible for the regularities observed in the built world. This allows architects to predict the functional and experiential outcomes of innovative forms that have no historical precedent.

## Concept: Integration and Choice (Closeness and Betweenness Centrality)
The very core of Space Syntax methodology relies on two primary measures derived from graph theory and network analysis: Integration and Choice. These measures quantify how any specific spatial element (a convex space or an axial line) is embedded within the global system.
---
**Integration (Closeness Centrality)** refers to the topological depth of a space. It measures how "close" a space is to all other spaces in the system. A space with high closeness centrality is "integrated"; it requires fewer steps to reach from any other point in the complex. Mathematically, it represents the potential for a space to be a destination for movement. In social terms, integration identifies the "core" of a building or city where encounter is most probable.
---
**Choice (Betweenness Centrality)** measures the degree to which a space lies on the shortest paths between all other pairs of spaces in the network. A space with high betweenness centrality has high "choice" value, meaning it has a high potential to be used as a through-route. While Integration highlights destinations, Choice highlights the potential for through-movement.
---
By adopting these network analysis terminologies, Space Syntax provides a mathematical language to describe the "energy economy" of a plan. The distribution of closeness and betweenness centrality creates an objective map of functional potentials. For example, retail often gravitates toward lines of high closeness centrality (integrated streets) to maximize accessibility, while through-traffic is predicted by high betweenness centrality (choice). Together, these measures allow for the identification of "inequality genotypes"—the deep structures of integrational and through-movement inequalities that define cultural and functional building types.

## Core Proposition: Generic Function (Intelligibility and Functionality)
Generic function refers to the most fundamental aspects of human use of space—occupation and movement—that are prior to any specific cultural or programmatic requirement. It acts as the "first filter" between the field of possibility and architectural actuality.
---
1. **Intelligibility**: The degree to which a complex can be understood from its parts. It is measured by the correlation between a space's local "connectivity" (what can be seen) and its global "integration" (its position in the whole system).
2. **Functionality**: The ability of a complex to accommodate movement and encounter. Integrated, depth-minimizing forms are generally more "well-formed" because they optimize the relation between occupation and movement, creating a "natural movement" pattern that supports social co-presence.

## Theoretical Context: The Organism-Environment Paradigm
Hillier critiques the "paradigm of the machine" and the "organism-environment paradigm" that dominated 20th-century social engineering in architecture. These paradigms assumed a direct, mechanistic relation between physical form and human behavior (architectural determinism), ignoring the mediating role of spatial configuration.
---
Space Syntax proposes a "Newtonian" shift: just as the principle of inertia describes motion as a state rather than a change, "natural movement" describes the distribution of people in a configuration as a result of the grid's own mathematical and morphological laws. The built environment is not a mere backdrop; it is a social behavior in itself.
