## Metaphor: The Gift Shop (Life Cycle Illustration)
Wicker uses the example of a gift shop to illustrate the four temporal stages:
*   **Preconvergence:** The owner has inheritance money (reserves) and sales experience (human resources), but no shop exists. Merchandise is in warehouses elsewhere.
*   **Convergence:** Space is leased, shelves are installed, and merchandise is ordered. Staff is hired and trained. This stage is characterized by high energy expenditure and assembling resources.
---
*   **Continued Existence:** The shop operates daily. Merchandise is displayed, bought, wrapped, and removed. Routines are established. Resources are modified piecemeal (e.g., replacing a clerk, restocking items).
*   **Divergence:** The shop is liquidated. Equipment is sold, clerks are terminated, and the space is vacated. The components disperse to other settings.

## Empirical Observation: Differentiation in Yoredale
[Context: Setting Growth]
To illustrate how settings grow and differentiate (split), Wicker cites a personal communication from Roger Barker regarding a barber shop in Yoredale, England.
*   **Original State:** A two-man barber shop also sold toiletries and antiques.
*   **Growth:** Demand for antiques increased. The shop was redesigned to dedicate one wall to sales.
---
*   **Differentiation:** Eventually, the neighboring room was rented, a display window was added, and a shop assistant was hired specifically for sales. The single setting differentiated into two distinct functional components, illustrating the transition from simple growth to structural differentiation.

## Critique of Traditional Theory: "Interchangeable People"
Wicker critiques Barker’s famous assertion that "people are anonymous; they are equipotential; their individuality is irrelevant to behavior settings."
*   **Counter-Argument:** Wicker argues that while newcomers must conform to broad outlines, they bring individual characteristics (traits, habits) that alter the setting.
*   **Founder Influence:** Founders are particularly non-interchangeable. Their specific cognitive "cause maps" and preconvergence experiences determine the initial structure of the setting. If a key founder leaves during the Convergence stage, the setting often fails.

===SECTION===
## Theoretical Connection: Negotiated Order Theory
[Context: Social Processes]
Wicker integrates Strauss’s (1978) "Negotiated Order Theory" to explain how setting programs are actually enacted.
*   **Mechanism:** Rather than automatic adherence to a program, individuals play an "active, self-conscious role." Order is maintained through daily, often tacit, negotiations regarding tasks and privileges.
*   **Application:** In a new setting (Convergence), the "ratio of constructed to preexisting realities" is high, meaning almost everything is negotiable. In a mature setting (Continued Existence), preexisting realities (traditions) limit the scope of negotiation.

## Raw Text Snippet: Behavior Settings as Eco-Behavioral Science
"Barker views behavior settings as small-scale social systems whose components include people and inanimate objects. Within the temporal and spatial boundaries of the system, the various components interact in an orderly, established fashion to carry out the setting's essential functions." (p. 614)

## Raw Text Snippet: Critique of Stability
"Behavior settings have traditionally been viewed as 'givens'—as intact, mature, fully functional systems... In the present framework, I take a more expanded temporal perspective that considers the creation, growth, differentiation, decline, and termination of settings." (p. 615)
