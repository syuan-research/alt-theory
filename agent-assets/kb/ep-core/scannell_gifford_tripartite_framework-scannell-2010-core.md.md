## Summary
Scannell and Gifford (2010) synthesize the diverse and scattered definitions of place attachment into a cohesive "tripartite" framework. This model, often referred to as the PPP (Person-Process-Place) framework, defines place attachment as a multidimensional bond between individuals (or groups) and their meaningful settings. The framework is structured around three core dimensions: the Person (the actor), the Psychological Process (the nature of the connection), and the Place (the object of attachment).

===SECTION===
## Dimension 1: The Person (The Actor)
The Person dimension addresses the question: "Who is attached?" Attachment is determined by the meanings assigned to a place, which can occur at two distinct but overlapping levels: the individual and the collective.
---
**Individual Level**: Attachment is rooted in personal connections, individual experiences, and "experience-in-place." It often involves personal milestones (e.g., where one met a partner) or memories that contribute to a stable sense of self and personal growth.
---
**Collective Level**: Attachment involves symbolic meanings shared among a group or community. This includes cultural, religious, or historical ties. Culture links members to places through shared values and symbols, while religious attachment elevates specific locations to "sacred" status, fostering a sense of cultural fealty and group identity.

===SECTION===
## Dimension 2: The Psychological Process
The Process dimension addresses: "How are affect, cognition, and behavior manifested in the attachment?" It draws parallels to other social psychological constructs like attitudes.
---
**Affective (Emotional)**: This is the core of the bond, often described as "topophilia" or love of place. It includes positive emotions like pride and well-being, but can also involve grief or "mourning" when a place is lost (displacement).
---
**Cognitive**: This involves the memories, beliefs, and knowledge that link a person to a place. It includes "place identity," where the physical environment is incorporated into the self-concept, and "place schemas," which are cognitive structures that help individuals organize information about familiar types of environments.
---
**Behavioral**: Attachment is expressed through actions, most notably "proximity-maintaining" behaviors. This includes the desire to remain close to a place, returning to it after absence (e.g., pilgrimages or homecoming), and the physical "reconstruction" of a place following disasters to restore familiarity.

===SECTION===
## Dimension 3: The Place (The Object)
The Place dimension addresses: "What is the attachment to, and what is the nature of this place?" This is divided into social and physical characteristics across various geographic scales.
---
**Social Place Attachment**: Individuals are attached to places that facilitate social relationships, group identity, and a "sense of community." The place serves as an arena for social interaction or a symbol of one's social group (e.g., a neighborhood where one's relatives live).
---
**Physical Place Attachment**: This focuses on the physical features of the environment. It can include "place dependence," where a setting provides specific resources or amenities to fulfill goals (e.g., a lake for a kite surfer), or attachment to natural/wild landscapes that represent "environmental identity."

===SECTION===
## Functions of Place Attachment
The PPP framework identifies several evolutionary and psychological functions that explain why these bonds form:
1. **Survival and Security**: Places provide biological necessities (food, water, shelter) and offer a "safe haven" for protection.
2. **Goal Support and Self-Regulation**: Places facilitate the pursuit of personal goals and provide restorative environments for self-reflection and stress relief.
3. **Continuity**: Places provide "place-congruent continuity," linking an individual's past, present, and future through physical representations of personal history.

===SECTION===
## 7. PPP in Relation to Other Frameworks
The PPP framework is designed to be more integrative and inclusive than previous models, which often focused on only one or two dimensions.
---
**Comparison with Process-Exclusive Models**: Frameworks like Fullilove’s (1996) focus almost entirely on the psychological process (displacement and grief), whereas the PPP framework adds the "Place" and "Person" dimensions to provide context.
---
**Comparison with Social-Exclusive Models**: Models by Woldoff (2002) or Hunter (1978) portray attachment as a purely social phenomenon. The PPP framework acknowledges these social ties but also validates attachment to physical features (e.g., nature, architecture), which these models often overlook.
---
**Comparison with Residential Models**: Shumaker and Taylor (1983) defined attachment primarily within residential contexts. The PPP framework expands this to include recreational settings, sacred sites, and national identities, arguing that attachment is not limited to where one lives.
---
**Comparison with Low & Altman (1992)**: While Low and Altman proposed a similar multidimensional view, they argued that the levels are "inseparable." The PPP framework differs by proposing that these dimensions, while overlapping, are distinct and can be measured separately to see their unique effects on other behaviors (e.g., pro-environmental action).

===SECTION===
## Theoretical Context: Heuristic and Applied Value
The PPP framework serves as a "portrait" of place attachment research, offering a classification system to organize future studies.
---
**Heuristic Value**: It allows researchers to explore which combinations of the three dimensions are most common. For example, does "individually-based physical attachment" lead to different pro-environmental behaviors than "collectively-based social attachment"?
---
**Applied Value**: In land-use planning and conflict resolution, the framework helps identify the diverse meanings a single place might hold for different factions. By unearthing whether a group’s attachment is social or physical, planners can better incorporate these meanings into successful management strategies.
