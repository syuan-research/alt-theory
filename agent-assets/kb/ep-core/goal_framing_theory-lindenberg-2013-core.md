## Summary
Goal-framing Theory posits that human decision-making and perception are governed by three overarching goals: hedonic (to feel good), gain (to improve resources), and normative (to act appropriately). The theory argues that these goals compete for the "foreground" of attention.
---
A central tenet is that norm-guided (pro-environmental) behavior is inherently precarious because the "normative" goal is a priori weaker than the self-interested "hedonic" and "gain" goals. Therefore, sustainable behavior requires chronic social support to keep the normative goal focal and prevent it from being displaced by background self-interest.

===SECTION===
## Concept: The Goal-Frame and Modularity
The theory suggests the mind is organized in a "modular" way, where the active goal (the goal-frame) determines what information is attended to, what knowledge is accessible, and what alternatives are considered.
---
**Focal vs. Background**: The dominant goal is "focal" (the goal-frame). The other two goals do not disappear; they remain in the background and can either support or undermine the focal goal. For example, if a normative goal is focal (e.g., "don't litter"), a strong background hedonic goal (e.g., "I'm lazy and the bin is far") can destabilize the normative frame.

===SECTION===
## Core Proposition: The Three Overarching Goals
1. **The Hedonic Goal**: The goal "to improve the way one feels right now." It sharpens sensitivity to pleasure, mood, pain, and effort. It is evolutionarily the most basic.
---
2. **The Gain Goal**: The goal "to guard and improve one's resources." It focuses on personal assets, status, and future planning.
---
3. **The Normative Goal**: The goal "to act appropriately." This evolved from the "social brain" and the need for group cooperation. It focuses on what one *ought* to do, observance of rules, and the expectations of others.

===SECTION===
## Core Proposition: The Precariousness of Norms
Norm-guided behavior is described as "precarious" because the normative goal requires constant support to remain focal. Without cues (like the presence of others or clean environments), the goal-frame tends to decay or shift toward self-interest (hedonic/gain).
---
**Cross-Norm Inhibition**: The violation of one specific norm can weaken the entire normative goal-frame, leading to the violation of completely unrelated norms (e.g., seeing graffiti can lead to littering or stealing). This occurs because the disorder signals a lack of respect for norms in general, lowering the weight of the normative goal.

===SECTION===
## Concept: Smart Norms and Moral Hypocrisy
"Smart norms" are abstract social rules that require interpretation to fit a situation (e.g., "be helpful" or "act sustainably").
---
While flexible, smart norms create "moral wiggle room." When the normative goal is weak or costs are high, individuals use this ambiguity to engage in **moral hypocrisy**: stretching the interpretation of the norm to justify self-interested behavior (hedonic/gain) while still maintaining a positive self-image.

===SECTION===
## Theoretical Context: Biospheric Values
In the context of environmental behavior, specific "biospheric values" (concern for nature/ecosystem) serve as a stable anchor for the normative goal-frame.
---
When these values are activated, they increase the "oughtness" (moral obligation) of environmental actions, helping the normative goal withstand the pressure of competing hedonic or gain goals.
