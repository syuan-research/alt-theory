## Summary
Maria Lewicka provides a comprehensive review of 40 years of place attachment research, identifying a significant imbalance in the field. While the "Person" dimension (individual differences) has been extensively studied, the "Place" (physical attributes) and "Process" (psychological mechanisms) dimensions remain underdeveloped. The review focuses heavily on the *context* of attachment—examining how community size, diversity, physical scale, and socio-demographic factors like ownership and residence length determine the strength and nature of the bond. Lewicka argues for a shift toward "place sciences" that incorporate environmental aesthetics and phenomenology to understand the physical-environmental context of human-place relations.

===SECTION===
## Context: Open vs. Closed Places and Community Diversity
A central debate in the context of place attachment is the distinction between places understood as "bounded entities" (classic view) versus "open crossroads" (globalized view). 
---
[Context: Urban Sociology]
The classic view (Relph, 1976; Tuan, 1974) defines place through historical continuity and boundedness—a "cozy place of rest" against a dangerous outside. In contrast, the modern view (Massey, 2004; Harvey, 1996) defines place as a source of social interaction and diversity. 
---
Empirical research on the context of diversity shows that neighborhood diversity (socio-economic or racial) often correlates with a decline in place attachment. Putnam (2007) and others suggest that ethnic diversity may undermine interpersonal trust and community attachment, leading residents to "hunker down" rather than form bonds. However, this is moderated by individual preferences; "urbanophilic" residents may thrive in diverse, open city contexts, while "urbanophobic" residents prefer homogeneous, traditionally closed communities.

===SECTION===
## Context: Gated Communities vs. Open Settlements
The context of "artificial closure" through gated communities presents a unique case for place attachment. 
---
[Context: Residential Architecture]
Gated communities are settlements inhabited by socially homogeneous residents, surrounded by walls or fences. Research (Wilson-Doenges, 2000; Kirby et al., 2006) indicates that differences in place attachment between gated and open settlements often disappear when matched for social status and residence length. 
---
In some contexts, gating may actually *decrease* attachment by weakening neighborhood ties or reducing residence time. The psychological effect of gating depends on the socio-demographic composition of adjacent settlements; closing a community in a highly differentiated area may increase a sense of security, but it does not necessarily foster a deeper emotional bond with the place itself.

## Context: The Hierarchy of Place Scales
Place attachment is contextualized within a concentric hierarchy of scales: home, neighborhood, city, region, country, and continent.
---
[Context: Scale and Identification]
- **Home**: The prototypical place and "main spatial anchor." It is a symbol of continuity, order, and self-identity.
- **Neighborhood**: Paradoxically the most studied scale, yet characterized by diffuse and arbitrary borders. It is often treated as the "optimal level of abstraction" for researchers, though residents may not define it similarly.
- **City**: Described by Tuan as "centers of meaning." Cities have clearer delineated borders than neighborhoods and are stable through time.
---
Research reveals a "curvilinear" or U-shaped relationship between scale and attachment strength: people often feel more attached to their home and their city than to the intermediate level of the neighborhood. Furthermore, despite globalization, attachments remain traditionally local; even mobile "elites" often maintain strong local sentiments alongside cosmopolitan identities.

## Context: Socio-Demographic Predictors (Residence Length and Ownership)
The most consistent contextual predictor of place attachment across hundreds of studies is residence length.
---
[Context: Temporal and Economic Factors]
Length of residence is the "unquestionable winner" in predicting attachment to neighborhoods and cities. The highest increments of attachment typically occur within the first year of residence, after which the slope of the curve flattens. 
---
**Ownership Status**: Homeowners consistently show higher attachment than renters. This is theorized through several mechanisms:
1. **Investment**: Emotional and financial investment in buying and decorating a dwelling.
2. **Control**: Ownership increases the sense of control over the living area, allowing for modifications that reflect the self.
3. **Cognitive Certainty**: Ownership provides the certainty of a permanent place to live, encouraging the development of "deep roots."

## Context: Physical-Environmental Predictors
Lewicka critiques the historical tendency to treat the physical environment merely as a "social container." Recent research has shifted toward identifying physical features as independent predictors of attachment.
---
[Context: Environmental Aesthetics]
- **Building Size**: Consistently found to be a negative predictor. High-rise buildings often weaken neighborhood ties and result in lower attachment compared to single-family dwellings or smaller residences. This may be due to "diffusion of responsibility" or a lack of control over semi-public spaces.
- **Access to Nature**: Access to green areas and parks is a primary predictor of residential satisfaction and attachment.
- **Aesthetics and Upkeep**: Perceived building aesthetics, cleanliness (lack of incivilities), and quietness are significant physical predictors.
---
Theoretical frameworks like Christopher Alexander’s "Pattern Language" suggest that certain physical designs (walkable cities, "sittable" chairs, legible streets) convert abstract spaces into meaningful places with high "attachment potential."

## Context: Social Capital and Security
The social context of a place is defined by the strength of community ties and the sense of security it affords.
---
[Context: Social Capital]
Lewicka distinguishes between two types of social capital that form the context for attachment:
1. **Bonding Social Capital**: Typical of closed, homogeneous local communities with strong ties.
2. **Bridging Social Capital**: Found in open, diverse places that are tolerant of strangers.
---
The "ideal environment" for attachment combines both—offering security/stability (bonding) and challenge/novelty (bridging). Furthermore, a "sense of security" is a consistent positive predictor; as long as a place is perceived as safe, residents are more willing to venture out and form the social contacts that lead to emotional bonding.

## Theoretical Context: Phenomenology and "Place-Ballet"
The review emphasizes the phenomenological context of place, where meaning is created through bodily movement and routines.
---
[Context: Meaning-Making Processes]
David Seamon’s (1980) concept of the "place-ballet" suggests that sense of place is created through "time-space routines"—automatized everyday activities like driving, meeting neighbors, or walking. 
---
These routines produce "existential insidedness," or the feeling of belonging within the rhythm of life in a place. When people are forced to move (involuntary relocation), the trauma stems from the destruction of these routines. The physical environment must support these "body-ballets" through regularity and variety to become a target of attachment. This aligns with "embodied cognition," where we learn the meaning of the world through what our bodies can *do* within a specific spatial context.

## Theoretical Context: Environmental Aesthetics and Order
To develop a true "theory of place," Lewicka suggests looking toward environmental aesthetics and the "nature of order."
---
[Context: Theoretical Gaps]
Current research often lacks a theory that connects emotional bonds to the physical side of places. Potential "intangible" physical features that facilitate attachment include:
1. **Legibility and Mapability**: How easily a setting can be understood and navigated (Lynch, 1960).
2. **Prospect-Refuge**: The dialectical nature of places offering both security and a view (Appleton, 1984).
3. **Wholeness**: Alexander’s (2002) fifteen principles of order (e.g., strong centers, boundaries, local symmetries) that define "life" in a physical space.
---
By integrating these architectural and aesthetic principles with psychological processes, researchers can move beyond "who" is attached to "why" certain physical contexts possess higher attachment potential than others.
