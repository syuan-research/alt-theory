## Summary
Social Cognitive Theory (SCT) posits that being human is characterized by **human agency**—the capacity to intentionally make things happen. This perspective rejects dualistic views of nature versus nurture, proposing instead a "triadic reciprocal causation" where personal factors (cognitive, affective, biological), behavioral patterns, and environmental events operate as interacting determinants. The theory emphasizes that people are not merely reactive products of their environment or biological impulses, but producers of their social systems and life circumstances through intentionality, forethought, self-reactiveness, and self-reflectiveness.

===SECTION===
## Concept: Human Agency
Agency is defined as the intentional act of making things happen. It is not a discrete entity located in a specific place but an emergent property of the mind involving self-development, adaptation, and self-renewal.
---
Agency operates through phenomenal and functional consciousness, where individuals purposefully access and process information to select, construct, regulate, and evaluate courses of action. It is distinct from mechanical input-output models or subpersonal neural network activations which lack the subjective experience of the "self" directing action.

===SECTION===
## Core Proposition: Triadic Reciprocal Causation
Human functioning is explained by a model of triadic reciprocal causation. This involves a dynamic interplay between three factors:
1.  **Internal Personal Factors**: Cognitive, affective, and biological events.
2.  **Behavioral Patterns**: The actions taken by the individual.
3.  **Environmental Influences**: The external social and physical context.
---
These factors influence one another bidirectionally. The relative strength of each factor varies depending on the activity and circumstances. This model rejects the idea of a reified social structure operating independently of people; instead, social structures are both created by human activity and, in turn, impose constraints and provide resources for human functioning.

===SECTION===
## Concept: The Four Core Features of Human Agency
The exercise of agency relies on four distinct core features:
1.  **Intentionality**: Representation of a future course of action to be performed. It is a proactive commitment to bringing about a future state, not just an expectation.
2.  **Forethought**: The temporal extension of agency. People motivate themselves and guide actions in anticipation of future events, setting goals and visualizing outcomes.
---
3.  **Self-Reactiveness**: The ability to regulate motivation, affect, and action. This involves self-monitoring, comparing performance against personal standards, and applying corrective self-reactions (self-sanctions or self-satisfaction).
4.  **Self-Reflectiveness**: The metacognitive capability to examine one’s own functioning, thoughts, and the meaning of life pursuits. This is where individuals verify the soundness of their thinking and evaluate their efficacy.

===SECTION===
## Concept: Self-Efficacy
Perceived self-efficacy—the belief in one's capability to exercise control over one's own functioning and environmental events—is the foundation of human agency.
---
It acts as a pivotal factor in the causal structure of SCT because it affects adaptation and change not only directly, but through its impact on other determinants (e.g., goal aspirations, outcome expectations, perseverance in the face of obstacles). Unless people believe they can produce desired results, they have little incentive to act or persevere.

===SECTION===
## Theoretical Context: Modes of Agency
SCT distinguishes among three modes of agency:
1.  **Direct Personal Agency**: The individual directly influences their own environment and outcomes.
2.  **Proxy Agency**: A socially mediated mode where individuals rely on others (who have resources, expertise, or power) to act on their behalf to secure desired outcomes.
---
3.  **Collective Agency**: People acting conjointly on a shared belief in their collective power to produce desired results. This is an emergent group-level property derived from the interactive dynamics of group members.

===SECTION===
## Theoretical Context: Environmental Structures
The environment is not a monolithic entity but consists of three gradations of changeability requiring different scopes of agency:
1.  **Imposed Environment**: Conditions that are thrust upon individuals, often requiring accommodation.
2.  **Selected Environment**: The parts of the environment that people choose to engage with (e.g., choosing a school or career), which then influences their development.
---
3.  **Constructed Environment**: Environments that are created or redesigned by agentic action. Through invention and social modeling, people construct physical and social conditions that enable them to overcome constraints.

===SECTION===
## Theoretical Relationship: Biosocial Coevolution
SCT rejects "one-sided evolutionism" where biology strictly dictates behavior. Instead, it proposes a bidirectional view of **biosocial coevolution**.
---
While biology provides the physical structures and potentialities (constraints), psychosocial influences operate through these resources to fashion adaptive behaviors. Furthermore, through agentic action (e.g., tool invention, biotechnology, environmental modification), humans create new selection pressures that shape their own biological evolution.

===SECTION===
## Concept: Moral Agency
Moral agency involves the self-regulation of conduct through the application of moral standards and self-sanctions. It has two aspects:
1.  **Inhibitive**: The power to refrain from behaving inhumanely.
2.  **Proactive**: The power to behave humanely and invest in the welfare of others.
---
Mechanisms of **Moral Disengagement** (e.g., diffusion of responsibility, dehumanization) allow individuals to selectively disengage self-sanctions, permitting them to behave inhumanely without experiencing guilt or self-censure.
