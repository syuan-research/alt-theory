## Empirical Study: Gall Bladder Surgery Recovery (Ulrich, 1984)

[Context: Surgical Recovery]

This landmark study examined the restorative effects of window views on patients recovering from gall bladder surgery.

**Methodology**: Patients were assigned to rooms either with a view of a small stand of trees or a view of a brown brick building wall.

**Results**: Patients with the nature view had significantly shorter post-operative hospital stays. They also received fewer negative evaluative comments in nurses' notes and took fewer doses of strong narcotic pain drugs compared to the wall-view group.

## Empirical Study: Heart Surgery Patients (Ulrich & Lunden, 1990)

[Context: Intensive Care]

A two-year study at Uppsala University Hospital in Sweden investigated the effects of visual stimulation on 166 open-heart surgery patients.

**Methodology**: Patients were randomly assigned to view:
1. A "serene" nature picture (water or trees).
2. An abstract picture (curvilinear or rectilinear).
3. A control condition (white panel or no picture).

**Results**: Patients exposed to the nature picture experienced significantly lower postoperative anxiety than the control and abstract groups. Those viewing abstract art showed higher anxiety and performed worse on visual/perceptual functioning tests.

## Specific Example: Psychiatric Ward Reactions to Art (Ulrich, 1986)

[Context: Mental Health Facility]

An investigation into the effects of wall art in a psychiatric ward at a Swedish hospital revealed that patients reacted differently based on art style.

**Findings**: Over 15 years, certain paintings elicited overt negative responses. Specifically, seven paintings—all featuring abstract or "chaotic" content—were physically attacked or destroyed by patients. In contrast, no paintings depicting nature or representative landscapes were ever targets of physical attacks.

## Empirical Study: TV as Negative Distraction (Simons & Ulrich, 1986)

[Scenario: Blood Donation]

This study examined the use of television in a blood donor clinic as a potential distraction.

**Methodology**: Blood donors were observed in a waiting room with television on vs. off. 

**Results**: Daytime television was found to be a *negative* distraction. Donors watching TV had higher heart rates and systolic blood pressure compared to those in a room with a nature mural and no TV. The uncertainty and high-arousal content of typical television programming acted as an additional stressor rather than a calming agent.

## Raw Text Snippet: Economic Implications

"Some of this research that has linked nature to health-related effects raises the possibility that supportive design can be credibly related to dollar savings in healthcare costs. For instance, the study of gall bladder surgery patients found that individuals with attractive window views required fewer moderate and strong analgesic injections." (p. 104)

## Raw Text Snippet: Definition of Supportive Design Goals

"By focusing on the concept of stress, a theory of supportive design can be developed that conceptualizes human impacts of design in ways that are related directly to scientifically credible indicators or interpretations of wellness." (p. 99)

## Specific Example: Kidney Dialysis Environments

[Context: Chronic Illness]

Patients with chronic kidney disease experience pronounced loss of control due to restrictive diets, complex medication, and the necessity of spending 3-5 hours in a clinic multiple times per week. 

**Design Implication**: Research funded by the National Institutes of Health is investigating how dialysis clinic design (visual privacy, controllable TV) can improve patient compliance and job satisfaction for staff who also suffer from high burnout rates due to the stressful environment.
