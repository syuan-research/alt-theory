===SECTION===
## Summary
Relational Restoration Theory (RRT) is a theoretical extension of individual-level restoration theories (ART and SRT) to the level of dyads and small groups. It proposes that nature experience contributes to health not only by restoring individual psychophysiological and cognitive resources but also by restoring "relational resources" held between people. RRT focuses on how environments permit and promote the supportive exchange necessary to bolster strained relationships and maintain interpersonal health.

===SECTION===
## Concept: Relational Resources
Relational resources are interpersonal aspects of a relationship that do not exist in one person alone. These include trust, love, respect, mutual understanding, shared goals, and a positive valuation of shared history.
---
These resources provide the basis for individual and joint action, enabling and motivating the exchange of support. Like individual cognitive resources, relational resources can become depleted through chronic stress, time pressure, and recurrent failures in supportive exchange.

===SECTION===
## Concept: Standing Arrangements
Standing arrangements refer to the stable, often deliberate interpersonal and spatio-physical structures that enable people to exchange support over long periods. 
---
Examples include the choice of a shared residence or routine activities like family dinners. These arrangements anticipate restoration needs; when they fail or become strained, they must be "restored" or adjusted to prevent further depletion of the individuals involved.

===SECTION===
## Core Proposition: Permitting and Promoting Relational Restoration
RRT distinguishes between environmental features that "permit" restoration and those that "promote" it within a social context.
---
An environment **permits** relational restoration by providing "distance" from the ordinary settings and demands of work or family, effectively enabling a supportive exchange without new distractions. It **promotes** restoration by offering features that draw thoughts away from demands (fascination) and affordances for mutually appreciated activities, which intensifies shared engagement and facilitates intimacy.

===SECTION===
## Core Proposition: Mechanism of Social Privacy Regulation
RRT emphasizes the regulation of social interaction (privacy regulation) as an integral process. Standing arrangements allow dyads to move between solitude and togetherness in restorative settings.
---
Satisfaction of personal needs for restoration may sometimes conflict with relational needs. However, in nature, the relaxation of social strictures and the presence of "soft fascination" allow for a congruence where individuals can restore their own resources while simultaneously caring for the relationship through shared experience.

===SECTION===
## Core Proposition: Reciprocity and Interdependencies
The success of relational restoration depends on norms of reciprocity and the interdependencies between experiences across different situations.
---
**Reciprocity**: Partners must assume responsibility for providing support while expecting it in return. Forgoing one's own restoration to enable a partner's can deepen the pool of relational resources.
**Interdependencies**: Present experiences in a setting are shaped by shared memories of past situations and the anticipation of future ones. Shared positive memories in a specific natural setting enhance its perceived restorative value for the dyad.

===SECTION===
## Theoretical Relationships: RRT vs. ART/SRT
While Attention Restoration Theory (ART) and Stress Recovery Theory (SRT) focus on an individual’s internal transactions with the environment, RRT situates these transactions within the ongoing supportive exchange between people.
---
RRT argues that restoration does not occur in a social vacuum. It acknowledges that having company can either enhance restoration (by providing safety and shared pleasure) or degrade it (if the social interaction itself is demanding). It treats "social support" as an adaptive resource subject to the same depletion-restoration cycle as directed attention or physiological arousal.
