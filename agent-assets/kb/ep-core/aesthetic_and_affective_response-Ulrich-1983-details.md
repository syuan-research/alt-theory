## Study: Roadside Scenery and Preference (Ulrich, 1977)
[Context: Landscape Assessment]
The author conducted a cross-cultural study using 53 unspectacular rural scenes shown to American suburbanites and Swedish university students.
---
**Results**: The rank-order correlation between the Swedish and American preference ratings was .88. High preference was consistently accorded to views with midrange complexity, high focality, and even ground textures. Low preference was linked to restricted depth and rough, "scruffy" ground surfaces that presented obstacles to movement.

## Study: Restoration Hypothesis (Ulrich, 1979a)
[Scenario: Examination Anxiety]
University students experiencing anxiety due to a course examination viewed color-slide presentations of either everyday natural scenes or urban views lacking nature.
---
**Findings**: Natural scenes triggered a clear pattern of restoration (reduced sadness and increased positive affect). Exposure to urban views was actually detrimental to emotional well-being on some dimensions. Interestingly, these differences occurred even though complexity levels for both sets of slides were equivalent.

## Study: Physiological Response and Alpha Waves (Ulrich, 1981)
[Context: Psychophysiological Restoration]
This study measured brain electrical activity (alpha waves) in unstressed individuals viewing nature with vegetation, nature with water, or urban environments.
---
**Results**: Alpha wave amplitude was significantly higher when subjects viewed nature (especially water) compared to urban slides. Since alpha is an indicator of cortical arousal associated with wakeful relaxation, the findings suggest that individuals felt more wakefully relaxed while viewing natural settings. Water scenes were also found to sustain attention more effectively than urban scenes.

## Study: International Replication of Preferences (Shafer and Tooby, 1973)
[Context: Cross-Cultural Agreement]
Researchers showed 100 photographs of diverse rural and wilderness landscapes to 250 campers of different nationalities in Scotland.
---
**Results**: The campers' preference ratings correlated very highly (.91) with ratings from American subjects in a previous study. The ranked order of scenes was nearly identical across nationalities, suggesting that aesthetic preferences for nature are not random or strictly idiosyncratic to culture.

## Empirical Details: Threat and Tension in Art (Ulrich & Zuckerman, 1981)
The authors explored the relationship between threat and preference using 52 slides of natural landscape paintings.
---
**Findings**: Results revealed a highly significant inverse relationship between liking ratings and tension scores. Paintings depicting stormy seas, avalanches, or steep cliffs (high tension/threat) were disliked, while those containing calm water surfaces were highly preferred.

## Raw Text Snippet: Definition of Instinct
"McDougall defined an instinct as: 'an inherited or innate psycho-physical disposition which determines its possessor to perceive, and to pay attention to, objects of a certain class, to experience an emotional excitement of a particular quality upon perceiving such an object, and to act in regard to it... or at least to experience an impulse to such action.'"
