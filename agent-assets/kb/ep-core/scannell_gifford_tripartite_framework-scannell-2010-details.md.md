===SECTION===
## Case Study: Displacement in Boston's West End (Fried, 1963)
Fried investigated a neighborhood redevelopment project that forced residents to relocate. 
---
**Results**: The residents experienced "grief" similar to the loss of a loved one. This study provided early evidence that place attachment is grounded heavily in the **Affective** (Process) dimension, characterized by feelings of sadness and longing following the loss of familiar social and physical structures.

===SECTION===
## Case Study: Post-Disaster Reconstruction (Xenia, Ohio)
Following a 1974 tornado, planners attempted to "rectify" previous planning flaws in Xenia by proposing new zoning maps.
---
**Behavioral Expression**: Local residents and businesses used their power to override planners, recreating the town to look exactly as it did before. This illustrates the **Behavioral** (Process) dimension of attachment, where "familiarity and use" took precedence over "rational" improvements. Similar behaviors were noted after earthquakes in Friuli, Italy (1982).

===SECTION===
## Study: Pro-Environmental Attitudes on the Appalachian Trail (Kyle et al., 2004)
This study examined how different dimensions of attachment influence perceptions of environmental problems.
---
**Results**: Hikers with a high sense of **Place Identity** (Cognitive Process) were more concerned about crowding and litter. However, hikers with high **Place Dependence** (Physical Place) perceived these same problems as less important. This demonstrates that specific dimensions of the PPP framework can have unique and even opposite effects on attitudes.

===SECTION===
## Study: Hydropower Opposition in Norway (Vorkinn & Riese, 2001)
This study looked at how spatial levels of attachment affect attitudes toward development.
---
**Results**: Residents strongly attached to **specific local areas** (High Specificity/Place dimension) tended to oppose a proposed hydropower plant. However, those attached to the **municipality as a whole** (Lower Specificity) were more likely to favor the development. This highlights the importance of the "Spatial Level" within the Place dimension.

## Raw Text Snippet: Place Identity Definition
Proshansky and colleagues coined the term "place identity" to describe the "physical world socialization of the self." (p. 3)
---
"This occurs when individuals draw similarities between self and place, and incorporate cognitions about the physical environment (memories, thoughts, values, preferences, categorizations) into their self-definitions." (p. 3)

## Empirical Detail: Attachment in Vulnerable Populations
Fried (2000) suggested that place bonds are often more intense among vulnerable populations, such as immigrants or those who are pregnant or sick. In these cases, the "clinging" behavior (Behavioral Process) is a response to the sense of safety and reduced risk provided by the significant place.

## Empirical Detail: Harlow’s Monkeys (1961)
The paper cites Harlow's study of rhesus monkeys to support the **Survival and Security** function. Monkeys preferred the cloth "mother" over the wire "mother" that provided food, especially when frightened. This is used as an analogy to show that place attachment forms not just for physiological needs (food), but for the "sense of security and comfort" (p. 5).
