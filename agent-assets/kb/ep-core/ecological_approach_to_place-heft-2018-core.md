## Summary
This theoretical framework expands the scope of Ecological Psychology beyond the perception of immediate affordances (objects and surfaces) to include the perception and utilization of "Place." Synthesizing J.J. Gibson's ecological theory with Roger Barker's concept of "Behavior Settings," Heft argues that human perception-action is fundamentally nested within collective social structures. For children to develop adaptively in human communities, they must learn not just where places are, but the normative social practices required to participate in them.

===SECTION===
## Theoretical Context: The Neglect of Place
Psychology has historically neglected the concept of "place" because it developed primarily as a laboratory science, in contrast to the "natural history" tradition of other biological sciences (which relies on naturalistic observation).
---
In traditional research, the laboratory is treated as a "neutral workspace" or an empty container devoid of psychological significance. The ecological approach rejects this view, asserting that behavior cannot be decoupled from the setting in which it occurs, as action is always situated within an organism-environment system.

===SECTION===
## Key Concept: Place (Gibsonian View)
In Gibson's ecological approach, "place" is defined in opposition to the abstract geometric coordinates of Euclidean or GPS space. A place is a perceivable region of an extended surface layout.
---
It has specific ecological properties:
1. **Affordances**: It is defined by its functional significance for an animal (e.g., a sleeping place, a hiding place).
2. **Nested Structure**: A place is included within more encompassing places (e.g., a den within a riverbank).
3. **Adjacency**: Locomotion consists of moving from one adjacent place to another. 

===SECTION===
## Key Concept: Behavior Settings (Barkerian View)
Roger Barker discovered that in human communities, "places" are best understood as "Behavior Settings"—dynamic, quasi-stable "standing patterns of behavior and milieu." 
---
A behavior setting (e.g., a classroom lesson, a Tuesday evening poker game, a pharmacy) is an emergent structure. It is not a physical enclosure that one enters, but a system that comes into existence through the interdependent, joint actions of individuals supported by environmental affordances. 
---
**Self-Regulation**: Behavior settings are dynamic systems. If the setting's integrity is threatened (e.g., a player is missing from a sports game), remaining participants will adjust their actions to redistribute functions and keep the setting operational.

===SECTION===
## Core Proposition: Participation as Situated Skill
Individuals do not merely "enter" a behavior setting; they *join* it as participants. This participation is a "situated skill" requiring "know-how" rather than conscious reflection or rule-following.
---
Behavior in settings cannot be scripted because conditions constantly change. Instead, participation requires "regulated improvisation" and attunement to the functional dynamics of the joint action-environment system. Through bodily practice, individuals acquire the *habitus* required to weave into the flow of the setting.

===SECTION===
## Theoretical Relationship: Expedient vs. Proper Action
J.J. Gibson differentiated between "expedient action" (actions driven purely by functional efficiency) and "proper action" (actions driven by custom, tradition, and normative social practices). 
---
**Mechanism**: Developing children learn affordances not just idiosyncratically, but through "interaction frames" guided by others. In this joint frame, children shift from expedient behavior (e.g., banging a spoon) to proper behavior (e.g., feeding oneself at a dining table). Places, therefore, have a moral dimension; they define "what ought to be done" and constrain human agency within acceptable cultural bounds.

## Empirical Study: The Midwest Day Records (Barker & Wright, 1955)
[Context: Naturalistic Observation of Child Behavior]
Roger Barker established a field research station in a small Midwestern town (population ~700) to observe children’s daily activities. Researchers recorded the activities of 20 children from the time they woke up until bedtime.
---
**Results**: Barker found that social inputs directed at a child were poor predictors of their behavior. Instead, a child’s actions were highly congruent with the specific "locale" they were in. 
Crucially, the variability of an *individual child's* actions across different settings over a day was greater than the variability of actions among *different children* located in the *same setting*. This proved that behavior settings heavily constrain and predict behavior.

===SECTION===
## Specific Example: The Language Lesson
[Context: Educational Setting]
A primary school language lesson is not defined by the four walls of the classroom. It is an event that emerges from the joint actions of the teacher and students at a specific time. 
---
While a researcher cannot predict exactly what a child will do at a specific micro-instant, the behavior setting rules out a vast range of actions (e.g., running, shouting, tossing a ball). The most probable behaviors are sitting, reading, and listening, demonstrating how the setting frames the affordances available.

===SECTION===
## Empirical Study: Perceiving Settings via Point-Light Displays (Heft et al., 2014)
[Context: Perceptual Learning]
The researchers tested if individuals can perceptually differentiate distinct behavior settings based solely on dynamic patterns of human action. They used point-light displays to animate the movements of participants in five different settings, stripping away all environmental features (milieu).
---
**Results**: Participants could successfully identify the settings based on the dynamic patterns alone. Settings with tightly constrained and coordinated actions (e.g., a basketball game) were identified more easily than less constrained settings (e.g., a bank lobby). This suggests that humans perceptually attune to the "structure from motion" of social practices.

===SECTION===
## Methodological Detail: Operationalizing Interdependence
Barker used "interdependence" to define the boundaries of a behavior setting. 
---
[Scenario: Community Commerce]
Consider a drugstore that contains a pharmacy and a retail section. Are these one behavior setting or two? 
If operations in the pharmacy have little bearing on the retail store (low interdependence), but high interdependence internally, they are two distinct settings, even though they share a building. If actions across the entire group mutually influence each other, it is a single setting.
