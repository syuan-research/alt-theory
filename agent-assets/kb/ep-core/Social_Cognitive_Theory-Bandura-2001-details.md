## Specific Example: The "Graceful Hippopotamus" (Intentionality)
To illustrate that human agency involves designed conception rather than just mechanical reaction to inputs, the author provides a whimsical example.
---
"Under the indefinite prompt to concoct something new... one can deliberatively construct a whimsically novel scenario of a graceful hippopotamus attired in a chartreuse tuxedo hang gliding over lunar craters while singing the mad scene from the opera Lucia di Lammermoor." This demonstrates the generative power of the mind to create unique events that do not exist in the immediate environment.

===SECTION===
## Specific Example: Hamlet (Intentionality vs. Outcome)
Bandura uses the example of Hamlet to distinguish between the intent of an action and its consequence.
---
Hamlet intentionally stabbed the man behind the tapestry, believing it to be the King. However, he discovered to his horror that he had killed Polonius. The killing of the hidden person was intentional (agency was exercised), but the specific victim (the outcome) was unintended. This illustrates that agency refers to the act done intentionally, not necessarily the successful achievement of the precise desired outcome.

## Empirical Context: Health and Self-Management
[Context: Health Psychology]
The paper highlights the shift from a disease model to a health model, emphasizing lifestyle habits.
---
"If the huge health benefits of these few lifestyle habits were put into a pill, it would be declared a spectacular breakthrough in the field of medicine." Through self-management of health habits (exercise, diet, stress reduction), people exercise control over their health status, reducing risks and living more productive lives.

## Empirical Context: Fortuity and Chance Encounters
[Context: Life Trajectories]
The author discusses how agency involves managing "fortuity" (chance events).
---
**Example 1**: A flight delayed by a storm leads to a chance meeting of two people at an airport, which eventuates in marriage and geographic relocation.
**Example 2**: A book editor enters a lecture hall, takes an empty chair, and later marries the woman he sat next to.
**Mechanism**: Agency is used to *capitalize* on chance. "Chance favors only the prepared mind" (Pasteur). People make chance happen by being inquisitive and venturesome, and they exploit it by having the skills and self-beliefs to utilize unexpected opportunities.

===SECTION===
## Empirical Context: Collective Efficacy in Society
[Context: Social Change]
The paper cites Gandhi as an example of self-efficacy serving communal rather than individualistic goals.
---
"Gandhi mobilized a massive collective force that brought about major sociopolitical changes. He lived ascetically, not self-indulgently." This challenges the dualistic view that self-efficacy breeds selfishness; rather, a strong sense of efficacy fosters high group effort and performance attainments.

===SECTION===
## Empirical Context: Cultural Variation in Aggression
[Context: Cross-Cultural Psychology]
To counter biological determinism regarding aggression, the author cites cultural variability.
---
**The Swiss**: Formerly the main suppliers of mercenary fighters in Europe, they transformed into a pacific society.
**The Vikings**: Once plunderers, Sweden transformed into a peaceable mediator nation following a constitutional change.
**American Quakers**: Fully immersed in the violent U.S. society, they adopt pacifism as a way of life.
These examples argue that while biology provides the *potential* for aggression, culture and ideology determine its expression.

## Raw Text Snippet: Control and The Internet
"In the past, students’ educational development was largely determined by the schools to which they were assigned. Nowadays, the Internet provides vast opportunities for students to control their own learning. They now have the best libraries, museums, laboratories, and instructors at their fingertips... Good self-regulators expand their knowledge and cognitive competencies; poor self-regulators fall behind." (p. 11)
