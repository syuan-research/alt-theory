===SECTION===
## Summary
This paper presents a psycho-evolutionary theory of stress recovery, proposing that humans have an unlearned, biologically prepared predisposition to respond positively to unthreatening natural environments. The theory asserts that exposure to natural content (vegetation, water) triggers a rapid, preconscious affective shift that reduces physiological arousal and fosters psychological restoration, whereas many urban environments can hamper or impede this recovery process.

===SECTION===
## Concept: Stress and Restoration
Stress is defined as the process by which an individual responds psychologically, physiologically, and behaviorally to situations that challenge or threaten well-being. This process involves cognitive appraisal, negative emotions (fear, anger, sadness), and physiological mobilization of bodily systems (cardiovascular, skeletomuscular).
---
Restoration, or stress recovery, is the process of responding to a stressor with numerous positive changes. This includes a shift toward more positively-toned emotional states, a reduction in negatively-toned feelings, and a decrease in the activity levels of physiological systems that were mobilized during the stress response.

===SECTION===
## Concept: Psycho-evolutionary Framework
Ulrich’s psycho-evolutionary framework postulates that immediate, unconsciously triggered emotional responses play a central role in the initial level of responding to nature. Unlike purely cognitive models, this theory suggests that affective reactions can occur in 400ms or less, preceding conscious processing.
---
These quick-onset reactions are adaptive, motivating approach or avoidance behaviors that foster survival. For unthreatening nature, the constellation of restorative responses includes attention/interest accompanied by liking and a reduction in physiological arousal.

===SECTION===
## Concept: Biological Preparedness
Biological preparedness refers to a biologically prepared readiness to quickly and readily acquire certain responses to stimuli that were relevant to survival throughout evolution.
---
While often applied to fear (e.g., snakes, heights), Ulrich extends this to positive restoration. Modern humans possess a preparedness to respond restoratively to natural configurations (like savannah-like settings or water) that favored well-being, while lacking such preparedness for urban or man-made contents.

===SECTION===
## Core Proposition: Content vs. Stimulation Levels
The theory proposes that the content of an environment (natural vs. human-made properties) is more decisive for restoration than the mere quantity of stimulation or information rate.
---
Even when urban environments have low complexity or low information rates, they may still impede recovery because they lack the biologically prepared natural elements that trigger the parasympathetic nervous system. Conversely, complex natural scenes can be restorative because their content is processed with relative ease by a brain evolved in such settings.

===SECTION===
## Theoretical Context: Parasympathetic vs. Sympathetic Systems
The framework distinguishes between the two branches of the autonomic nervous system. The sympathetic system is responsible for the energy-consuming "fight or flight" mobilization during stress.
---
In contrast, restoration is characterized by parasympathetically dominated responding. This involves heart rate deceleration, sustained but non-taxing perceptual intake (attention), and the restoration of bodily energy resources.
