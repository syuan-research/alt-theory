===SECTION===
## Summary

The Theory of Supportive Design proposes that healthcare environments should be designed to foster coping with stress to promote wellness. Traditional "hard" facility designs often prioritize functional delivery but create psychological obstacles. This theory identifies three specific design strategies—Sense of Control, Social Support, and Positive Distraction—that act as resources to mitigate the major stress accompanying illness and hospitalization.

===SECTION===
## Core Proposition: The Concept of Supportive Design

Supportive design is defined by its capacity to reduce stress and facilitate recovery by creating physical surroundings that are "psychologically supportive." 

The theory rests on three pillars:
1. Healthcare facilities should not raise obstacles to coping with stress.
2. Environments should be designed to facilitate access to physical features and social situations that have stress-reducing influences.
3. Target groups for these interventions include patients, visitors, and healthcare staff.

===SECTION===
## Key Concept: Sense of Control

Sense of control refers to an individual's ability to influence their physical and social surroundings. In healthcare contexts, a lack of control is a pervasive problem that increases stress. 

Design can mitigate this by providing:
- Access to visual and physical privacy.
- Controllable televisions and lighting.
- Wayfinding systems that reduce the stress of being lost.
- Break areas for staff to escape work demands.

===SECTION===
## Key Concept: Social Support

Patients derive important benefits from frequent contact with family and friends who are helpful and caring. High social support is consistently linked to lower stress levels and higher wellness indicators.

Design facilitates social support by:
- Providing convenient overnight accommodations for families.
- Creating comfortable waiting areas with movable seating to allow family groupings.
- Designing outdoor gardens or sitting areas that foster interaction.

===SECTION===
## Key Concept: Positive Distraction

A positive distraction is an environmental element that elicits positive feelings, holds attention without requiring effort, and blocks worrisome thoughts. 

Characteristics of positive distractions include:
- Nature elements (trees, plants, water).
- Happy, laughing, or caring human faces.
- Animals.
- Multisensory engagement (sights, sounds, and smells).

===SECTION===
## Theoretical Context: Evolutionary Predispositions

The theory posits that modern humans have a biologically prepared readiness to quickly acquire restorative, stress-reducing responses to unthreatening natural settings. This is based on human evolution, where natural environments (like savannahs or water sources) provided survival advantages. Consequently, nature is not just a preference but a biological requirement for optimal stress recovery.

===SECTION===
## Exclusions: Negative Distractions

The theory distinguishes positive distractions from negative ones. Negative distractions are environmental elements that assert their presence, are difficult to ignore, and are characterized by uncertainty or high intensity (e.g., loud noise, flickering lights, or stressful television content). These features are more likely to be harmful if the patient is already stressed and needs calming.
