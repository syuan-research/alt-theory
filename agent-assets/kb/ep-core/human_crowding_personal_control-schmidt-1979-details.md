## Study: Perceived Control and Task Performance (Sherrod, 1974)
[Context: Laboratory Experiment]
Sherrod manipulated high and low density and added a "control" condition where subjects could choose to leave the dense room.
---
**Results**: Subjects in the high-density condition without control showed significant decrements in performance on complex cognitive tasks and less persistence on unsolvable puzzles later.
---
**Finding**: The mere *perception* of control (the option to leave, even if not exercised) was sufficient to ameliorate the negative aftereffects of high-density settings.

## Study: Locus of Control and Personal Space (Schopler & Walton, 1974)
[Context: Personality and Environment]
The authors investigated how internal vs. external locus of control affects the feeling of being crowded.
---
**Finding**: "Internals" felt less crowded than "externals" when situational control was structured and predictable. However, when the environment was unstructured (loss of control), internals reported higher levels of perceived crowding because their need for efficacy was thwarted.

## Study: Dormitory Design and Social Control (Valins & Baum, 1974)
[Context: Residential Environment]
Research compared students living in corridor-style dormitories versus suite-style dormitories.
---
**Findings**: Corridor-style residents (exposed to more unwanted social contact) rated their dormitories as more crowded. They felt less control over their social environment and developed a "learned helplessness" regarding social interactions.
---
**Coping**: Suite-style dormitories provided better "shielding" from unwanted stimulation, allowing residents to maintain higher cognitive and decisional control over who they interacted with.

## Study: Supermarket Cognitive Overload (Langer & Saegert, 1977)
[Context: Public/Consumer Environment]
Participants were asked to perform grocery tasks in high and low-density supermarkets. Some were given prior info about the arousal they might feel.
---
**Results**: Participants in the high-density supermarket felt less uncomfortable and crowded when they were given "Information Gain" (cognitive control).
---
**Mechanism**: Prior warning allowed them to attribute their arousal to the environment beforehand, reducing the psychological strain of the causal search during the task.

## Empirical Study: Animal Social Anomalies (Calhoun, 1962)
[Context: Behavioral Sink]
Calhoun’s research on rat colonies is cited to establish the baseline for density-related pathology.
---
**Observed Behaviors**: Under extreme density, rats exhibited "socially deviant" behaviors including cannibalism, homosexuality, and the total disruption of maternal duties (nest building and nursing).
---
**Physiological Data**: Christian (1960) found that mass mortality in dense deer populations was linked to enlarged adrenal glands, a hallmark of chronic stress, rather than disease or malnutrition.

## Raw Text Snippet: Attributional Labeling
"Crowding is an attributional label applied to a setting when situational density results in a loss or lack of personal control... Crowding occurs only when the individual's lack of control is attributed to the presence of others." (p. 680-681)

===SECTION===
## Raw Text Snippet: Definition of Decisional Control
"Decisional control refers to the ability of the individual to select outcomes or goals in a social situation... in the crowding literature, decisional control is tantamount to behavioral control, since restricted behaviors and blocked goals functionally limit the activities that are possible." (p. 693)
