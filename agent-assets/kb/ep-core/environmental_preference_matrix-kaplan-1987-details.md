## Empirical Study: The Content Effect (Kaplan et al., 1972)
[Context: Initial Model Testing]
This early study involved sampling scenes across different complexity levels and environments.
---
**Results**: The study found an overwhelming "content effect." Natural scenes were uniformly preferred over built environments, even when the natural scene was a simple "urban park" and the built environment was architecturally complex. Complexity, while predicted to be a linear driver, played no predictive role across the 56 scenes studied, leading to the search for other predictors like Mystery.

## Empirical Study: Biome Preferences (Balling & Falk, 1982)
[Context: Evolutionary Testing]
The researchers studied the preferences of individuals of different ages for various biomes: desert, rain forest, savanna, mixed hardwoods, and boreal forest.
---
**Findings**: Young children (ages 8 and 11) showed a substantial preference for the savanna over all other biomes. As participants aged (up to 15), preference for more familiar biomes (like hardwoods in the Eastern US) rose to meet the savanna level.
---
**Significance**: This supports the hypothesis of an innate, evolutionary bias toward the savanna—the environment in which the human species is believed to have evolved—which is later modified by experience and familiarity.

## Empirical Study: Landscape Predictors (Woodcock, 1982)
[Context: Testing the Preference Matrix]
This study tested the predictive power of the Matrix variables (Mystery, Legibility, Coherence, Complexity) alongside Appleton’s Prospect and Refuge theory.
---
**Methodology**: A panel of judges rated 72 scenes on a 5-point scale. The scenes represented three biomes: savanna, mixed hardwoods, and rain forest.
---
**Results**: Mystery, Legibility, and "Primary Prospect" were all found to be strong predictors of preference. Interestingly, Mystery and Legibility interacted: Mystery was a strong predictor when Legibility was also high, but its effect decreased in scenes where the observer might feel lost.

## Theoretical Detail: Prospect and Refuge (Appleton, 1975)
[Context: Environmental Theory Integration]
Jay Appleton proposed that preferred landscapes provide "Prospect" (an overview/grand view) and "Refuge" (a safe place to hide).
---
**Integration**: Kaplan’s framework incorporates these as adaptive advantages. Woodcock’s empirical work further refined these into "Primary" (immediate view) and "Secondary" (view from a distance) versions. The most preferred "Safe" environments were found to be at the forest edge, where one has both a clear view of the open area (Prospect) and the immediate availability of cover (Refuge).

## Raw Text Snippet: The Definition of Mystery
"Mystery, as we intended it, refers to instances when the new information is not present, but is inferred from what is in the scene. It is unlikely that the new information predicted in a high-Mystery setting will be totally surprising when it is experienced, since there tends to be continuity in such settings between what can be seen and what is inferred." (p. 9)
