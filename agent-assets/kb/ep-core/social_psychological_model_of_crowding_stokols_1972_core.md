===SECTION===
## Summary
Stokols proposes a heuristic model of human crowding that distinguishes between physical density and the psychological experience of crowding. The theory positions crowding as a multivariate stress syndrome resulting from the interaction of spatial, social, and personal variables. It moves beyond a purely spatial view, suggesting that crowding is a state where an individual's demand for space exceeds the available supply, triggering a cycle of psychological or physiological strain and subsequent coping responses.

===SECTION===
## Concept: Density vs. Crowding
A fundamental distinction is made between the physical condition and the psychological experience.
---
**Density**: A physical condition involving the limitation of space. It is a univariate condition of limited space without inherent motivational overtones.
---
**Crowding**: A psychological experience of stress resulting from the perception of spatial inadequacy. It is a multivariate phenomenon characterized by the interaction of spatial, social, and personal factors, resulting in adverse manifestations of stress.

===SECTION===
## Concept: Social vs. Nonsocial Crowding
Crowding is categorized based on the source of spatial restriction.
---
**Nonsocial Crowding**: A person's supply of usable space is restricted to an inadequate level by purely physical factors (e.g., cramped quarters, cluttered environments).
---
**Social Crowding**: Awareness of spatial restriction is related directly to the presence of other persons and the individual's relationship to them. It involves social constraints and competition for scarce resources (space and materiel).

===SECTION===
## Core Proposition: The Equilibrium Model

The model operates as a functional cycle involving four basic dimensions: Environmental Qualities (E), Personal Attributes (P), Experience of Stress (S), and Response to Stress (R).
The relationship between input (E, P) and output (R) factors is one of equilibrium. The system consists of:
Environmental Qualities (E): Divided into Physical (amount/arrangement of space, stressors like noise, glare, temperature, and pollution) and Social (status/power allocation, division of labor, and interference from competition or coordination problems).
Personal Attributes (P): Includes Momentary States (hunger, sexual arousal), Idiosyncratic Skills (intelligence, strength, agility), and Personality Traits (Locus of Control, Comparison Level, and Type A-B patterns).
The interaction of these variables induces an Experience of Stress (S), manifesting Psychologically (cognitive inconsistency, feelings of infringement, or alienation) and Physiologically (disequilibrium in blood pressure, temperature, or hormonal secretion).
A response (R) is adaptive if it effectively adjusts the environment (4a) or personal attributes (4b) to relieve strain and break the stress cycle. It is maladaptive if it intensifies strain and perpetuates the cycle (5a, 5b). Extremes in one variable are compensated for through adjustments in others to maintain balance between the individual and the environment.

===SECTION===
## Core Proposition: Psychological Reactance and Cognitive Inconsistency
[Context: Motivational Mechanisms]
Psychological stress in crowding manifests in two primary forms: cognitive inconsistency and emotional imbalance.
---
**Cognitive Inconsistency**: The recognized disparity between an individual's supply of and demand for space. This awareness provokes "psychological reactance"—a motivational state involving feelings of infringement and behavior directed toward reestablishing threatened freedom.
---
The intensity of reactance depends on the importance of the freedom of behavior eliminated and the degree of restriction attributed to the presence of others.

===SECTION===
## Theoretical Context: Levels of Analysis
Crowding phenomena can be addressed at two distinct levels:
---
**Macrocosmic (Sociological)**: Focuses on the effects of large-scale urban population density on societal and cultural integration.
---
**Microcosmic (Psychological)**: Primarily concerned with the impact of perceived spatial restriction on the individual's behavior and internal stress levels. This model focuses on the microcosmic level.
