## Summary
This theory articulates a specific typology of nine human values of nature, grounded in the **Biophilia Hypothesis**. The central premise is that human identity and personal fulfillment are biologically dependent on a relationship with the natural world. This dependence extends beyond material exploitation to include emotional, cognitive, aesthetic, and spiritual development. The author argues that these nine values—Utilitarian, Naturalistic, Ecologistic-Scientific, Aesthetic, Symbolic, Humanistic, Moralistic, Dominionistic, and Negativistic—are not merely cultural constructs but reflect universal, evolutionarily adaptive learning rules. The degradation of this relationship leads to a diminished human existence, suggesting that nature conservation is essential for human mental and physical well-being.

===SECTION===
## Concept: The Typology of Biophilia Values (Overview)
Kellert proposes nine distinct perspectives or "values" that describe the human relationship with nature. While the intensity and content of these values may vary across cultures and individuals, their underlying forms are hypothesized to be universal and rooted in human evolutionary history.
---
Collectively, these values function to enhance human fitness by providing physical sustenance, fostering cognitive development, ensuring security, facilitating social bonding, and offering spiritual meaning. They represent a "cluster of learning rules" rather than rigid instincts.

===SECTION===
## Core Propositions: Values of Direct Experience and Knowledge
These values relate to how humans physically utilize, explore, and understand the natural world.

*   **Utilitarian**: This value focuses on the practical and material exploitation of nature. It encompasses the physical benefits derived from nature, such as food, medicine, tools, and shelter. It is the fundamental basis for human sustenance and security.
*   **Naturalistic**: This value is characterized by satisfaction derived from direct contact with nature. It involves fascination, wonder, and awe at nature's diversity. Evolutionarily, it drove exploration and the acquisition of outdoor skills (climbing, tracking), contributing to physical fitness and mental well-being (stress mitigation).
*   **Ecologistic-Scientific**: While distinct, both perspectives focus on systematic study. The *Scientific* view is often reductionist, focusing on morphology and mechanical functioning. The *Ecologistic* view is more integrative, focusing on the interdependence of biotic and abiotic elements. Both reflect a biological urge for precise observation and understanding of the environment.
*   **Dominionistic**: This value reflects the desire to master and control the natural world. While often associated with destruction in the modern era, evolutionarily it relates to the survival skills needed to subdue prey and survive in an adversarial environment. It fosters physical prowess and mechanical skills.

## Core Propositions: Values of Meaning, Affection, and Symbolism
These values relate to the emotional, communicative, and spiritual connections humans forge with nature.

*   **Aesthetic**: The appreciation of the physical beauty of nature. This response is thought to be ancient, potentially guiding humans toward environments offering safety and resources (e.g., water, vegetation). It provides feelings of harmony, symmetry, and order.
*   **Symbolic**: The use of nature as a means to facilitate communication and abstract thought. Nature provides a vast taxonomy of forms used for language development, metaphor, and categorization. It is critical for the development of selfhood and expressive thought.
*   **Humanistic**: This value involves deep emotional attachment and "love" for individual elements of nature (often sentient animals). It fosters capacities for bonding, altruism, and cooperation, which are essential for a social species.
*   **Moralistic**: A perspective encompassing spiritual reverence, ethical responsibility, and a sense of kinship with nature. It seeks order and meaning in life, linking human identity to a larger, integral natural system.
*   **Negativistic**: Characterized by fear, aversion, and antipathy (e.g., toward snakes or spiders). Evolutionarily, this value served a vital safety function by causing humans to avoid dangerous or noxious elements of the environment.

## Theoretical Relationship: Biophilia and Conservation Ethics
The framework suggests a shift in the rationale for nature conservation. Rather than relying solely on altruism or material sustainability, conservation is framed as a matter of "profound self-interest."
---
Because the human psyche is "shaped by the earth," a degraded environment leads to a degraded human experience. Therefore, preserving biodiversity is necessary not just for economic reasons, but to maintain the potential for a fulfilling emotional, intellectual, and spiritual life.
