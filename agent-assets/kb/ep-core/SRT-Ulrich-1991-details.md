## Study Methodology: The Two-Stage Video Experiment
[Context: Laboratory Simulation]
The study used 120 undergraduate volunteers who were first subjected to a stressor before being randomly assigned to one of six recovery environments.
---
**Stressor Phase**: Participants viewed a 10-minute black-and-white film ("It Didn't Have to Happen") depicting serious woodworking injuries with simulated blood and mutilation.
**Recovery Phase**: Participants then viewed one of six 10-minute color/sound videotapes: two natural (Forest/Vegetation and a fast-moving Stream) and four urban (Heavy Traffic, Light Traffic, Many Pedestrians, and Few Pedestrians).

===SECTION===
## Empirical Results: Physiological Stress Indicators
[Scenario: Post-Stressor Recovery]
The researchers measured four physiological indicators: Skin Conductance (SCR), Muscle Tension (EMG), Pulse Transit Time (PTT/Systolic Blood Pressure), and Heart Period (HP).
---
**Recovery Rate**: Recovery was significantly faster and more complete in the natural settings. For example, greater recovery in terms of SCR occurred within the first few minutes for the nature groups. PTT and EMG differences became significant during the 5–7 minute interval and persisted throughout the 10-minute exposure.
**Urban Performance**: High stimulation urban environments (heavy traffic) produced the least recovery, but even low-stimulation urban settings (light traffic) were significantly less restorative than nature.

===SECTION===
## Empirical Results: Heart Rate and Attention
The study analyzed Heart Period (HP) as an indicator of intake and attention. A longer heart period indicates a slower heart rate (deceleration).
---
**Findings**: Nature tapes elicited heart rate deceleration, even after the initial deceleration caused by the stressor film. This indicates high levels of sustained, non-taxing attention. In contrast, urban exposures (both pedestrian mall and traffic) caused heart rate acceleration throughout the recovery period, returning to baseline levels, which indicates less perceptual intake and higher arousal.

===SECTION===
## Empirical Results: ZIPERS Self-Ratings
Psychological states were measured using the Zuckerman Inventory of Personal Reactions (ZIPERS) before and after the experiment.
---
**Fear and Anger**: Nature groups showed significantly lower scores for the Fear and Anger/Aggression factors compared to urban groups.
**Positive Affect**: The most salient difference was the surge in Positive Affect scores for the nature groups. By the end of the recovery period, the nature groups reported feeling even more positively-toned than they did during the baseline period before the stressor.

## Raw Text Snippet: Preconscious Response
"Findings from several studies using exposures of several seconds have found that well-defined positive or negative emotional responses to natural stimuli... can occur in 400 ms or less. This very rapid emotional/physiological responding... is difficult to reconcile with a purely 'controlled' cognitive perspective." (p. 207)

===SECTION===
## Raw Text Snippet: Biological Preparedness Quote
"Modern humans might have a biologically prepared readiness to quickly and readily acquire restorative responses with respect to many unthreatening natural settings, but have no such preparedness for most urban or built contents and configurations." (p. 208)

===SECTION===
## Empirical Findings: Evaluation of Arousal Theory
The results challenged traditional arousal theory. Arousal theory predicted that urban settings with high stimulation (heavy traffic) would elicit more attention than nature, but the heart rate data showed the opposite.
---
Furthermore, there was no general relationship between the quantity of stimulation and recovery; low-stimulation urban scenes did not foster significantly better recovery than high-stimulation urban scenes, whereas nature (even with movement/sound) consistently outperformed both.
