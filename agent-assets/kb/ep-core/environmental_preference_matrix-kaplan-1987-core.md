===SECTION===
## Summary
This paper proposes a theoretical framework for environmental preference based on human evolutionary history. It argues that aesthetic reactions are not merely casual or trivial but serve as an intuitive guide to behavior, helping individuals choose environments that are likely to support survival and effective functioning. The framework organizes environmental predictors into a "Preference Matrix" based on two primary informational needs: Understanding (making sense of an environment) and Exploration (being attracted to new information).

===SECTION===
## Evolutionary Basis of Preference
From an evolutionary perspective, environmental preference is an expression of an intuitive guide to behavior. Humans are motivated to seek out and prefer environments where they can function effectively and avoid those where they would be at a disadvantage.
---
This biological substrate suggests that preferences are adaptive; they entice people toward environments that provide necessary resources while allowing for the updating of cognitive maps. Survival depended on the ability to process information about the landscape quickly and accurately, leading to the development of rapid, unconscious affective judgments.

===SECTION===
## The Preference Matrix: Informational Outcomes
The framework identifies four key variables that predict environmental preference, categorized by the time required to process them (Immediate vs. Inferred) and the cognitive goal (Understanding vs. Exploration).
---
**Understanding (Making Sense)**:
- **Coherence (Immediate)**: The ease with which a scene can be organized and comprehended.
- **Legibility (Inferred/Predicted)**: The ability to orient within the space and find one's way if one were to wander deeper into the scene.

**Exploration (Attracted to Information)**:
- **Complexity (Immediate)**: The richness or diversity of elements in a scene that keeps one engaged.
- **Mystery (Inferred/Predicted)**: The promise that more information could be gained by moving deeper into the environment.

===SECTION===
## Concept: Coherence
Coherence refers to the immediate ease of organizing and comprehending a scene. It is enhanced by factors such as symmetry, repeating elements, and unifying textures that contribute to a "good gestalt."
---
A coherent scene allows the information to be organized into a relatively small number of "chunks." Environments that lack redundancy or are difficult to organize are typically rated low in preference because they frustrate the human need to make sense of the surroundings.

===SECTION===
## Concept: Complexity
Complexity, originally termed "richness" or "diversity," refers to the amount of information immediately available in a scene. It is determined by the number and variety of different things to look at.
---
While early research focused on a simple optimal level of complexity (an inverted-U relationship), the integrative framework views complexity as one of several factors that drive the desire to explore. A scene must be complex enough to be worth engaging with, but not so chaotic that it destroys coherence.

===SECTION===
## Concept: Legibility
Legibility is the predicted or inferred aspect of Understanding. It concerns the individual's ability to remain oriented in space and to find their way back.
---
A legible environment is one that appears as if it could be easily explored without getting lost. This is often supported by distinct landmarks and a clear spatial structure. Legibility allows for "wayfinding" and suggests that the environment is safe for movement.

===SECTION===
## Concept: Mystery
Mystery is the predicted or inferred aspect of Exploration. It occurs when a scene provides a promise of additional information if the observer were to walk deeper into the landscape.
---
Common elements that evoke mystery include a path that bends around a corner or a brightly lit clearing partially obscured by foliage. Mystery is distinct from "surprise" because the new information is not present yet; it is inferred based on the continuity of the setting. It is one of the most powerful and consistent predictors of preference in natural environments.

===SECTION===
## Theoretical Synthesis: Cognition and Affect
The framework challenges the dichotomy between cognition and affect by showing that aesthetic reactions are the result of rapid, complex information processing.
---
Preferences represent a "primary landscape quality" where the role of content (e.g., water, foliage) and the role of informational structure (the matrix variables) interact. This synthesis suggests that aesthetics is a functional part of human cognition, serving as a rapid assessment tool for environmental fitness.
