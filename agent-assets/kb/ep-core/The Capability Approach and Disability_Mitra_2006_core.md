## Summary
This paper assesses the utility of Amartya Sen's Capability Approach (CA) as a conceptual framework for understanding disability, particularly in the context of economic causes and consequences. It first reviews established models of disability—the Medical Model, the Social Model, the Nagi Model, and the International Classification of Functioning, Disability and Health (ICF). The paper then positions the Capability Approach as an integrative framework that defines disability not merely as a medical impairment or social oppression, but as a deprivation of capabilities (practical opportunities) resulting from the interaction of personal characteristics, resources, and the environment. This perspective allows for the analysis of disability at two distinct levels: *potential disability* (capability deprivation) and *actual disability* (functioning deprivation), while also highlighting the economic dimensions often overlooked by other models.

## Concept: The Medical Model of Disability
The Medical (or Biomedical) Model views disability as a direct attribute of the individual, caused specifically by disease, trauma, or other health conditions.
---
**Core Mechanism**: The problem is located within the individual's body or mind. It posits a normative standard of "normal" functioning; individuals are considered disabled because they cannot function as a "normal" person does. The individual is placed in the "sick role," where the condition is unwanted.
---
**Response Paradigm**: The primary response is medical care, treatment, and rehabilitation. The goal of intervention is to "fix" the individual—to bring them back to or close to the norm. Political and social concerns are largely limited to the provision of healthcare services.
---
**Critique**: This model is strongly normative and treats disability as a personal tragedy rather than a socio-political issue. It ignores the environmental and social barriers that contribute to the experience of disability.

===SECTION===
## Concept: The Social Model of Disability
The Social Model, contrasting sharply with the medical model, asserts that disability is not an attribute of the individual but a social construct created by an oppressive environment.
---
**Core Mechanism (UK Version)**: Originating from the Union of the Physically Impaired Against Segregation (UPIAS), this model distinguishes between *impairment* (physical/mental limitation) and *disability* (social exclusion). Disability is imposed on top of impairments by a society that isolates and excludes people. It is inherently political, viewing disability through a Marxist lens of societal oppression.
---
**Core Mechanism (Oppressed Minority Version)**: Common in the US, this variation posits that people with disabilities face discrimination, segregation, and barriers (architectural, sensory, attitudinal) similar to those faced by racial or ethnic minorities. It emphasizes civil rights and the removal of barriers.
---
**Response Paradigm**: The solution requires social change, not individual medical treatment. This involves removing physical barriers, changing attitudes, and restructuring society to be inclusive.

## Concept: The International Classification of Functioning (ICF)
The ICF, developed by the WHO, attempts to synthesize the medical and social models into a "biopsychosocial model." It provides a standard language and framework for describing health and health-related states.
---
**Core Mechanism**: Disability is not a single entity but an interaction between health conditions (diseases, disorders) and contextual factors.
*   **Body Functions and Structures**: Physiological and anatomical status (where *impairments* occur).
*   **Activity**: The execution of a task or action by an individual (where *limitations* occur).
*   **Participation**: Involvement in a life situation (where *restrictions* occur).
*   **Contextual Factors**: Including environmental factors (social attitudes, legal structures, terrain) and personal factors (gender, age, coping styles).
---
**Qualifiers**: The ICF uses two distinct qualifiers to assess disability:
1.  **Capacity**: An individual’s ability to execute tasks in a *standardized* environment (neutralizing environmental impact).
2.  **Performance**: What an individual actually does in their *current* environment (lived experience).
This distinction brings the ICF conceptually close to the Capability Approach.

## Concept: The Capability Approach (CA)
Amartya Sen's Capability Approach is a framework in welfare economics for assessing well-being, poverty, and freedom. It shifts the focus from "opulence" (income/commodities) and "utility" (happiness/pleasure) to what people are actually able to do and be.
---
**Key Concept: Functionings**: These are the "beings and doings" of a person. They can range from elementary biological states (being well-nourished, escaping premature mortality) to complex social activities (taking part in the life of the community, having self-respect).
---
**Key Concept: Capability**: This represents "practical opportunity." A capability set is the combination of functionings a person *can* achieve. It reflects the real freedom a person has to choose the life they value.
*   *Example*: Two people may both be hungry (same functioning deprivation). One is fasting for religious reasons (has the capability to eat but chooses not to); the other is starving due to poverty (lacks the capability). Their well-being status is fundamentally different despite identical functionings.
---
**Role of Commodities**: Resources and goods are merely means to an end. Their value depends on the individual's "conversion factors" (personal characteristics like metabolism or impairment) that allow them to convert goods into functionings.

## Theoretical Application: Disability in the Capability Approach
In the CA framework, disability is analyzed as a deprivation of capabilities or functionings resulting from the interaction of three distinct factors:
1.  **Personal Characteristics**: Impairment (physiological/mental loss), age, gender, race.
2.  **Resources**: Assets, income, goods available to the individual.
3.  **Environment**: Physical, social, economic, and political context.
---
**Potential Disability**: A restriction in the *capability set*. Due to an impairment (interacting with the environment/resources), a person has a reduced range of practical opportunities compared to others. This is an objective reduction in freedom.
---
**Actual Disability**: A restriction in *functionings*. An individual is actually disabled if they cannot do or be the things they value. This is subjective and dynamic; a person might adapt their preferences ("adaptive preferences") or acquire "compensating abilities," thereby not considering themselves disabled despite an impairment.

## Theoretical Relationship: CA vs. Medical and Social Models
The Capability Approach offers a broader evaluative space than traditional disability models.
---
**Comparison with Medical Model**: The Medical Model focuses narrowly on the impairment and bodily function. It measures disability against a "normal" body standard. The CA accepts impairment as a personal characteristic but argues that disability only arises when that impairment interacts with resources and environment to restrict opportunities. The CA rejects the normative "sick role" and focuses on the expansion of freedom.
---
**Comparison with Social Model**: The Social Model locates disability entirely in the environment (social oppression). While the CA agrees that environment is crucial, it argues the Social Model oversimplifies by ignoring personal characteristics and resources.
*   *Critique*: A wealthy person with an impairment has different capabilities than a poor person with the same impairment. The Social Model treats them largely the same (both oppressed), whereas the CA recognizes that economic resources can mitigate disability (e.g., buying a car to overcome lack of public transport). The CA integrates the economic dimension of disability—how impairments act as "earning handicaps" and "conversion handicaps" (needing more income to achieve the same standard of living).

===SECTION===
## Theoretical Relationship: CA vs. The ICF
The ICF is the closest existing model to the Capability Approach, particularly in its distinction between "Capacity" (similar to Capability) and "Performance" (similar to Functioning).
---
**Similarities**: Both acknowledge that disability arises from the interaction of the individual and the context. Both view disability as a multidimensional experience beyond just medical health.
---
**Differences/Limitations of ICF**:
1.  **Scope of Functioning**: The ICF limits "functioning" to health-related domains. The CA's definition is broader, including non-health goals (e.g., political participation, self-respect) which are vital to well-being.
2.  **Economic Context**: The ICF does not explicitly account for economic resources as a constitutive factor of disability. It treats socioeconomic factors as external "context." The CA explicitly models how a lack of resources (poverty) can *cause* disability and how disability creates a need for extra resources.
3.  **Operationalization**: While the ICF provides a classification system, the CA provides a normative framework for justice and policy, highlighting that society has an obligation to expand capabilities, not just classify limitations.
