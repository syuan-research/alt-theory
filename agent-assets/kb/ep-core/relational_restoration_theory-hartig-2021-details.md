## Scenario: The Couple in the Forest
[Scenario: Relational Restoration in Nature]
Hartig illustrates RRT through a scenario of a couple walking in an unfamiliar forest. The experience is shaped by their "standing arrangements" and shared history of recreational activity.
---
1. **Distance**: Getting away from work demands permits them to attend to one another.
2. **Shared Engagement**: They search for a specific orchid together. Their shared curiosity and the eventual delight of discovery amplify the intensity of the pleasure (shared attention).
3. **Outcome**: The absence of other people and social strictures makes self-disclosure and emotional expression easier, resolving undiscussed matters and reaffirming trust.

## Empirical Study: Safety and Company (Staats & Hartig, 2004)
[Context: Perceived Restoration Likelihood]
Participants judged the likelihood of restoration during a forest walk shown in slides, comparing being alone versus being with a close friend while either "fatigued" or "fresh."
---
**Results**: Participants generally appreciated company. Crucially, "greater safety" was found to mediate the positive effect of company on restoration, but only for the forest walk (not the urban walk).
---
**Finding**: The study suggested that if safety were guaranteed, participants saw a higher likelihood of restoration if alone. This highlights how "company" acts as a permitting feature of the environment in RRT.

## Empirical Evidence: Shared Attention and Pleasure
The text cites research (Boothby, Clark, & Bargh, 2014) regarding the amplification of experiences.
---
**Evidence**: When two people in a close relationship enjoy a positively valenced stimulus together (e.g., viewing pleasant images), the intensity of the pleasure each receives is amplified, even without explicit communication. This supports the RRT claim that "sharing of the experience intensifies engagement" with restorative settings.

## Application: Nature-Based Therapies
[Context: Clinical Intervention]
RRT principles have been applied in practical settings to bolster family and couple dynamics.
---
- **Couples Therapy**: Nature-guided therapy (Burns, 2000) uses natural settings to help relationships "dawn anew."
- **Youth Programs**: Outdoor activities (Davidson & Ewert, 2012) are designed to promote the development of relational resources between parents and children.
- **Wilderness Programs**: Designed to enlist personal restorative processes to develop social outcomes like cooperation and communication (Ewert & McAvoy, 2000).

## Methodology: Measuring Distal Outcomes
Research focused on the distal outcomes of RRT (long-term effects) must consider the characteristics of "standing arrangements."
---
**Proposed Methods**: 
1. **Behavioral Observation**: Studying solo vs. shared restorative activities in daily life (Saxbe et al., 2011).
2. **Surveys**: Examining how family members reciprocate in permitting each other's "periodic withdrawal" into preferred restorative activities and how this relates to marital stability.
