## Summary
Allan Wicker presents a significant elaboration and extension of Roger Barker’s traditional Behavior Setting Theory. While retaining the core concept of the behavior setting as a bounded, self-regulating system, Wicker critiques the traditional view for treating settings as static "givens" with interchangeable people.
---
Wicker proposes a dynamic life-cycle framework that views settings as social constructions. This framework analyzes settings across four temporal stages (Preconvergence, Convergence, Continued Existence, and Divergence) and examines them through three facets: Resources (expanded to include information and reserves), Internal Dynamics (incorporating individual cognition and negotiation), and Context (historical and environmental linkages).

===SECTION===
## Concept: Behavior Setting (Revised Definition)
A behavior setting is a small-scale social system composed of people and inanimate objects interacting within specific temporal and spatial boundaries to carry out essential functions. 
---
Unlike the traditional view which sees settings as homeostatic and deterministic entities independent of specific individuals, Wicker’s revised definition views them as evolving social constructions. They are "standing crews" or naturally occurring groups with an indefinite life expectancy, distinct from temporary groups (like experimental subjects) or broad natural groups (like families).

===SECTION===
## Core Proposition: The Temporal Stages of Behavior Settings
Wicker proposes that behavior settings are not static but pass through a life cycle consisting of four distinct stages:
1.  **Preconvergence:** The stage before the setting exists as a distinct entity. Resources exist but are dispersed in the larger social environment.
2.  **Convergence:** The birthing stage where resources are assembled, configured, and organized into a functional program. This involves high negotiation and trial-and-error.
---
3.  **Continued Existence:** The mature, quasi-stable stage (the focus of traditional theory) where the program is enacted repeatedly. However, this stage involves continuous adaptation, growth, and differentiation rather than strict homeostasis.
4.  **Divergence:** The decline or termination of the setting. Resources are uncoupled and dispersed back into the environment or repurposed for other settings.

===SECTION===
## Core Proposition: Five Types of Resources
For a setting to function, it requires specific resources. Wicker expands the traditional list (people, objects, space) to include two abstract categories necessary for development and adaptation:
1.  **Human Resources:** Staff with motivation and working knowledge, and clients/consumers.
2.  **Behavior Objects:** Tools, equipment, supplies, and raw materials.
3.  **Space:** Bounded physical areas with appropriate ambient conditions (synomorphy).
---
4.  **Information:** Knowledge regarding operations, history, and external linkages (essential for the convergence and adaptation stages).
5.  **Reserves:** Uncommitted resources (money, slack time, credit) available for future use or to absorb shock (essential for survival during crises or growth).

===SECTION===
## Mechanism: Internal Dynamics and Social Processes
Traditional theory relies on "maintenance circuits" to correct deviations. Wicker argues that internal dynamics are driven by **cognitive** and **social processes**.
*   **Cognition:** Settings depend on the "cause maps" or mental representations held by occupants (especially founders). Newcomers may view setting constraints as objective realities ("the only way"), while founders view them as constructed choices ("our way").
*   **Negotiation:** Functional activities (who does what) are not automatic but are the result of "Negotiated Order." Participants actively bargain over tasks based on personal motives and vested interests.
*   **Organizing:** Following Weick’s model, occupants engage in cycles of acting, interpreting, and retaining information to reduce uncertainty and stabilize the setting program.

===SECTION===
## Theoretical Context: Setting Context and Linkages
Settings do not exist in isolation. They are embedded in a larger social, cultural, and economic context.
*   **Setting History:** The specific events preceding a setting's establishment (Preconvergence) shape its subsequent development and internal culture.
*   **Linkages:** Settings are tied to other settings through resource exchange (inputs/outputs) and social influence. For example, a setting may depend on outside authorities for legitimacy or on supplier settings for raw materials.
