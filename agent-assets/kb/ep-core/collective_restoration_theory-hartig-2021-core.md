## Summary
Collective Restoration Theory (CRT) extends the restoration perspective to the level of communities and populations. It moves beyond individual (ART/SRT) and dyadic (RRT) analysis to focus on the restoration of social resources held collectively. CRT posits that just as individuals experience fatigue, entire populations can experience aggregated resource depletion due to societal stressors. Restoration at this scale is enabled by institutional arrangements—such as laws and customs—that regulate time and provide shared access to restorative settings, fostering collective outcomes like optimism and social trust.

===SECTION===
## Concept: Collective Social Resources
CRT identifies social resources as the bond and capacity for cooperation among people, including those who do not know each other personally. These resources are often discussed in terms of "social capital."
---
These resources include broad social trust, mutual respect, and a collective sense of optimism about the future. They underlie the implicit cooperation of citizens toward common ends, such as the maintenance of public services and civic engagement.

===SECTION===
## Concept: Aggregated Depletion
Aggregated depletion occurs when stressors impact a large proportion of a community simultaneously, or when individual and relational fatigue scales up to affect the social structure.
---
Causes of collective depletion include circumstances that shake the public’s trust in institutions (e.g., political crises, pandemics) or the destruction of shared environments (e.g., war, displacement) that serve as bases for community identity.

===SECTION===
## Core Proposition: Institutional Regulation of Time
A central tenet of CRT is that collective restoration is permitted through the "social regulation of time." This involves institutional provisions that allow large segments of a population to simultaneously withdraw from demands.
---
Examples include the Judeo-Christian Sabbath, national holidays, and vacation legislation. These laws and customs create "temporal structures" that facilitate the convergence of people in restorative settings, ensuring that restoration is not just an individual choice but a socially supported right.

===SECTION===
## Core Proposition: Permitting and Promoting Collective Restoration
CRT distinguishes between features that allow a population to rest and those that actively build collective resources.
---
**Permitting Features**: Institutional arrangements that mandate the relaxation of demands on a large scale (e.g., a standard four-week summer vacation).
---
**Promoting Features**: Provisions for population access to suitable restorative settings (e.g., national park systems) and arrangements for engagement in shared activities that foster "conviviality" and positive emotional contagion among those present in public places.

===SECTION===
## Core Proposition: Emotional Contagion and Shared Identity
Restoration at the collective level often depends on a setting being populated with people who are collectively freed from mundane demands.
---
When a group experiences restoration together, a "contagion of mood" becomes likely. This spread of positive affect among known and unknown individuals works to renew and reinforce communal identity and collective optimism.

===SECTION===
## Theoretical Context: Sociocultural and Opportunity Structure Paradigms
CRT situates the adaptive process within two broader research paradigms.
---
1. **Opportunity Structure Paradigm**: Focuses on the spatio-physical and temporal characteristics of settings that serve the pursuit of population-level goals.
2. **Sociocultural Paradigm**: Views the challenge of survival not as an individual concern but as a problem for the social structure (family, neighborhood, or nation) in which the individual is embedded.
