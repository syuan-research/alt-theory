## Summary
This theoretical framework challenges the dominant "spectator" approach to landscape perception, which treats the environment as a static image to be viewed from a distance. Drawing on J.J. Gibson's ecological psychology, the author argues for an action-based perspective where perception is an active, embodied process. The central concept of **Affordances** is applied to environmental aesthetics, suggesting that aesthetic experience is rooted in the functional possibilities and active engagement offered by a landscape, rather than merely its visual pictorial qualities.

===SECTION===
## Theoretical Context: The Spectator vs. The Agent
Traditional environmental psychology often operates under an "interactionist" worldview, which treats the person and the environment as separate, dualistic entities. This view assumes perception acts like a camera—capturing static retinal images that the mind must then process subjectively.
---
**The Spectator Stance**: Consequently, research has historically focused on stationary observers looking at landscapes (or photographs of them) as detached spectators. This leads to a "picturesque" view of aesthetics, where value is placed on visual composition rather than function.
---
**The Ecological Alternative**: The ecological approach proposes a "transactionalist" worldview. It argues that organisms are fundamentally animate and that perception is a continuous, dynamic process designed to guide action. One cannot understand perception without understanding the ongoing life processes of the organism within its niche.

===SECTION===
## Key Concept: Perceptual Systems
Perception is not a passive reception of stimuli but an active "perception-action" process. Organisms constantly move (turning heads, walking, manipulating objects) to generate information.
---
**Mechanism**: Action facilitates perception. For example, moving through an environment produces a changing optical array that reveals the invariant structure of the world. Therefore, the "immediately present" environment is not a static snapshot but a continuous flow of information revealed over time through exploration.

===SECTION===
## Core Proposition: Affordances
**Definition**: Affordances are the functional properties of the environment taken in relation to a specific individual. They represent what the environment "offers, furnishes, or provides" for good or ill.
---
**Relational Nature**: Affordances cut across the subjective-objective dichotomy. They are objectively real physical properties, yet they are defined only in relation to the biological and physical scale of a specific user.
---
**Implication**: Meaning is not imposed by the mind onto a meaningless physical world; meaning (function) is directly perceived in the relationship between the observer's capabilities and the environment's features.

===SECTION===
## Theoretical Application: The Aesthetics of Engagement
The framework proposes shifting environmental aesthetics from a "detached appreciation" (liking how something looks) to an "aesthetics of engagement" (the quality of the interaction).
---
**Attraction and Valence**: Environmental features possess "physiognomic" qualities that seduce or repel action. A landscape is aesthetically pleasing not just because it is pretty, but because it invites skillful engagement (e.g., a tree that invites climbing, a path that invites walking with buoyancy).
---
**Design Implication**: Designers should view landscapes as "arenas for action" rather than static scenes. Aesthetic value is found in how well the environment supports, promotes, and sustains the life activities of its users.
