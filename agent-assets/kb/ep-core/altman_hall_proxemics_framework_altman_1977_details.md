## Study: Spatial Invasion and Flight (Felipe & Sommer, 1966)

[Context: Mental Health Facility]

Researchers studied reactions to inappropriate closeness by having a confederate sit 6 inches away (shoulder-to-shoulder) from mental patients.

**Results**: The invaded subjects fled much faster than non-invaded controls. Approximately one-third left within 2 minutes, and two-thirds fled within 20 minutes. Invaded patients also showed nonverbal discomfort through fidgeting, postural shifts, and rubbing their bodies.

## Study: Library Seating Invasion (Patterson et al., 1971)

[Scenario: Public Study Space]

Confederates in a college library sat at varying distances from unsuspecting students: adjacent, two seats away, three seats away, or across the table.

**Findings**: There was a direct correlation between the closeness of the intrusion and the speed of flight. The closer the confederate sat, the more the students exhibited "blocking" behaviors (elbows out) and the more rapidly they vacated their seats.

## Study: Urinal Invasion and Physiological Response (Middlemast et al., 1976)

[Scenario: Men's Lavatory]

This study examined the impact of spatial intrusion on a physiological function: urination. Confederates stood either at an adjacent urinal or one further away from the subject.

**Results**: Closer spatial invasion resulted in slower latencies of onset of urination and shorter urination durations. This provided empirical evidence that proxemic violations can have measurable physiological and inhibitory effects.

## Empirical Finding: Standing vs. Seated Distances

The review of 106 studies revealed a significant discrepancy between standing and seated interactions that Hall did not explicitly address.

**Data Patterns**:
People in standing arrangements typically use the far phase of the intimate zone (6-18 inches) or the close phase of the personal zone (18-30 inches).
People in seated arrangements typically use the far phase of the personal zone (2.5-4 feet) or the near phase of the social zone (4-7 feet).
This suggests that being seated inherently increases the physical distance maintained between individuals.

## Study: Cultural Distance at the Zoo (Baxter, 1970)

[Context: Ethnic Subcultures]

Baxter observed the distancing behavior of different ethnic groups viewing exhibits at a zoo.

**Findings**: Mexican-Americans stood closest to one another, whites were intermediate, and blacks maintained the greatest distance. However, these results were influenced by interactions with age, sex, and setting (e.g., blacks were closer in indoor settings, while Mexican-Americans were closer in outdoor settings).

===SECTION===
## Study: Socioeconomic Status vs. Race (Scherer, 1974)

[Context: Educational Environment]

Scherer examined black and white school children to see if distancing was a function of race or class.

**Findings**: Middle-class children (both black and white) stood further apart than lower-class children. This suggests that socioeconomic status and related variables may be more powerful determinants of proxemic behavior than ethnicity alone.
