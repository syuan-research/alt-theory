===SECTION===
## Summary
Schmidt and Keating propose a control-attribution model of human crowding. They argue that crowding is not a direct result of physical density, but rather an **attributional label** applied to a setting when situational density results in a perceived loss or lack of personal control. The framework integrates previous research on behavioral constraint (social interference) and cognitive overload, suggesting that the experience of crowding is mediated by three types of personal control: behavioral, cognitive, and decisional.

===SECTION===
## Concept: Density vs. Crowding
A critical distinction is maintained between the physical state and the psychological evaluation.
---
**Population Density**: A strictly physical index defined as the number of people per unit area.
---
**Functional Density**: A measure of the number of others within a setting who directly affect an individual's behaviors and perceptions.
---
**Crowding**: A cognitive evaluation and negative affective reaction to the environment. It is a subjective experience triggered when density-related factors interfere with an individual’s goals or psychological state.

===SECTION===
## Core Proposition: The Three Types of Personal Control (Averill's Model)
The framework uses Averill’s (1973) distinctions to explain how individuals interact with dense environments:
1. **Behavioral Control**: The ability to choose or modify actions to deal with aversive stimuli. In dense settings, this is often restricted by "Social Interference," where others block goal-directed behaviors.
---
2. **Cognitive Control**: The way events are interpreted or appraised. This includes "Information Gain" (predicting stressors) and "Appraisal" (evaluating the situation). Crowding occurs when the individual cannot effectively process or filter the overwhelming social stimuli (Stimulus Overload).
---
3. **Decisional Control**: The ability to select among various goals or outcomes. Crowding is perceived when density limits the options available to an individual, essentially stripping them of the freedom to choose their desired level of social interaction.

===SECTION===
## Core Proposition: The Control-Attribution Model
The model suggests a chronological sequence leading to the experience of crowding:
1. **Situational Density** leads to either Behavioral Constraint (blocked goals) or Cognitive Overload (excessive stimulation).
---
2. These states result in a **Loss or Lack of Personal Control** over the environment.
---
3. The individual experiences **Arousal/Stress** and conducts a "Causal Search."
---
4. If the individual identifies density as the salient cause of this loss of control, they apply the **Attributional Label** of "Crowding."

===SECTION===
## Theoretical Context: Locus of Control and Self-Efficacy
The framework integrates personality variables into the crowding response. An individual's "Locus of Control" (Rotter, 1966) dictates their susceptibility to density.
---
**Internals** (those with high perceived self-efficacy and control) may feel more crowded as density increases because they have a higher threshold for maintaining control and feel the restriction more acutely.
---
**Externals** (those who believe outcomes are determined by outside forces) may be more adaptable to high-density stress because they do not expect to exert high levels of personal control over the environment initially.

===SECTION===
## Discussion on Environment: Imposed vs. Selected
The framework emphasizes that the environmental context determines the severity of the crowding experience.
---
**Primary Environments** (e.g., home, work): Settings where the individual spends significant time and performs important activities. Here, goal blocking and social interference are more critical, and the lack of control is most distressing.
---
**Secondary Environments** (e.g., shopping malls, transportation): Settings that are transitory. Individuals utilize "Cognitive Strategies" like filtering or social withdrawal (Milgram, 1970) to manage overload, making the lack of control less psychologically damaging than in primary settings.
