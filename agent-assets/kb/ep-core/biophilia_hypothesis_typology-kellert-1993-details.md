## Empirical Study: Cross-Cultural Perspectives (USA vs. Japan)
[Context: Comparative Cultural Analysis]
The author contrasts the "nature-loving" reputations of the United States and Japan with empirical findings from his surveys.
---
**Findings**: Despite Japan's cultural reputation for revering nature (Shintoism, bonsai, tea ceremony), the general public in Japan revealed limited knowledge and ecological understanding of nature compared to Americans. Japanese appreciation was often restricted to a small number of valued species (idealized "semi-nature") and emphasized control and manipulation.
**Contrast**: Americans showed a somewhat more generalized interest, particularly among the young and educated, though they also favored large vertebrates over lower life forms. Both populations showed limited appreciation for species lacking aesthetic or cultural value.

## Empirical Evidence: The Negativistic Value
[Context: Phobias and Aversion]
The text cites research supporting the biological basis of fear toward specific natural elements.
---
**Evidence**: Studies by Ulrich et al. (1991) and others show that images of snakes or spiders can elicit pronounced autonomic responses (fear) even when presented subliminally.
**Interpretation**: This supports the evolutionary argument that "active escape and avoidance" systems were among the first functional behaviors to evolve. The aversion to invertebrates is linked to their alien ecological strategies, "monstrous" forms, and radical autonomy from human will.

## Specific Example: The Symbolic Value in Childhood
[Context: Language Acquisition]
To illustrate the *Symbolic* value, Kellert cites data regarding the prevalence of animal characters in early childhood education.
---
**Data Point**: Animals constitute more than 90 percent of the characters employed in language acquisition and counting in children's preschool books.
**Implication**: This supports the claim that nature provides the "code images" by which humans develop speech, abstract thought, and classification skills.

## Empirical Evidence: Childhood Cruelty and Adult Violence
[Context: The Consequences of Degraded Biophilia]
The author cites his own research (Kellert & Felthous, 1985) regarding the link between animal abuse and human violence.
---
**Finding**: Significant abusers of nature, specifically those who inflict willful harm on animals during childhood, are far more likely in adulthood to reveal repeated patterns of violence and aggressive behavior toward other people. This supports the hypothesis that a degraded relationship with nature correlates with a diminished moral and social existence.
