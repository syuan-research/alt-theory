## Study: National Vacation Legislation and Mental Health (Hartig et al., 2013)
[Context: Public Health Policy]
This study served as an initial test of CRT by analyzing the mental health consequences of national vacation legislation in Sweden.
---
**Methodology**: The researchers used aggregate monthly data on vacation-taking and the dispensation of anti-depressants to the entire Swedish population over a 12-year period (1993–2005).
---
**Results**: Modeling uncovered a significant negative association between vacationing and the aggregate dispensation of anti-depressants. 
**Collective Spread**: Crucially, the benefit held for both people in the workforce and those of retirement age. This suggests that when workers take vacations, the restorative benefits spread through the community, even to those not currently working.

## Study: Collective Optimism and Population Indicators (Catalano et al., 2020)
[Context: Social Resource Assessment]
This study addressed "collective optimism" as a social resource and form of "informal insurance" that members of a population draw upon.
---
**Measures**: To observe optimism on a population level, the researchers tracked two variables in Sweden from 1973 to 2016:
1. **Suicide rates among women of reproductive age**: Representing a conscious decision to stop investing in the future.
2. **Incidence of male twin births**: Representing non-conscious biological "decisions" regarding the investment of reproductive capability in vulnerable gestations.
---
**Findings**: These two seemingly disparate measures were found to covary reliably over decades, suggesting a shared population-level state of optimism or despair that impacts both behavior and biology.

## Specific Examples: Conviviality and Shared Spaces
Hartig lists several real-world examples where the restorative quality of a setting depends on the collective mood of its occupants.
---
- **Times Square**: New Year’s Eve celebrations.
- **Memorial Stadium (Lincoln, Nebraska)**: Cheering crowds at sports events.
- **Vatican City**: The Papal blessing on Easter in St. Peter’s Square.
- **Grateful Dead Concerts**: Historic large-scale gatherings that opened channels for emotional contagion and shared identity.

## Specific Example: Post-War Urban Forest Restoration
[Context: Restoration of Social Identity]
The text mentions how the restoration of lost social resources can occur through the physical recreation of damaged environments.
---
**Scenarios**: Efforts to restore urban forests in cities that sustained heavy war damage—such as Tokyo, Hiroshima, Sarajevo, Hamburg, and Dresden—served as restorative processes that involved both private and institutional actors working to rebuild a shared sense of place and community attachment.

## Raw Text Snippet: Social Regulation of Time
"Unlike the other theories... CRT takes particular interest in the social regulation of time. It recognizes that by regulating time for different activities, laws, and customs affect the ability of people to converge in particular settings and social constellations for activities that support restoration of individual, relational, and more diffuse social resources." (p. 122)

## Raw Text Snippet: Environmental Predictability
"CRT recognizes that the social regulation of time attends to well-known and highly predictable variations in the natural environment that have implications for visiting and enjoying outdoor settings... The theory assumes that settings which support restoration are located not only in space but also in time." (p. 122)

## Contextual Detail: COVID-19 and Institutional Trust
Hartig notes a contemporary example of resource depletion at the collective level.
---
**Scenario**: The COVID-19 pandemic served as a massive stressor that either boosted or undermined the public’s trust in political leadership and health institutions. CRT posits that the vital importance of this trust constitutes a collective resource, and when it weakens, it creates a state of population-level depletion.
