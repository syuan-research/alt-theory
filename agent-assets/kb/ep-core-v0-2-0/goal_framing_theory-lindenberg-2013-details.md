## Study: Cross-Norm Inhibition (The Graffiti Study)
[Context: Urban Disorder]
The authors cite Keizer, Lindenberg, & Steg (2008) to illustrate how the violation of one norm spreads to others.
---
**Scenario**: In an environment where the anti-graffiti norm was clearly violated (graffiti was present), people were observed.
**Result**: Subjects were significantly more likely to litter (violate a cleanliness norm) and even steal (violate a property norm) compared to a clean environment. This supports the claim that signs of disorder weaken the general normative goal-frame.

## Study: The Watching Eyes Effect
[Context: Honesty/Cooperation]
Citing Bateson, Nettle, and Roberts (2006), the authors discuss how cues of "other people" strengthen the normative goal.
---
**Experiment**: In a coffee/tea room where payment was based on honesty, a poster was displayed featuring either flowers or a pair of eyes looking at the observer.
**Result**: The poster with eyes almost tripled the amount of money paid compared to the flower poster. This demonstrates that cues of social observation—even artificial ones—can activate the normative goal-frame.

## Study: Unreturned Shopping Carts
[Context: Parking Garage]
Another experiment by Keizer et al. (2010) is described to show how the behavior of others influences littering.
---
**Setup**: Flyers were placed on windshields in a parking garage. The environment contained unreturned shopping carts (a violation of a specific setting norm).
**Result**: People who saw the unreturned carts littered the flyer significantly more often than those who did not see the carts. The visual cue that "others are not conforming" reduced the weight of the normative goal for the observers.

## Mechanism: Moral Wiggle Room (Batteries Example)
The authors provide a scenario illustrating "smart norms" and moral hypocrisy.
---
**Scenario**: A general norm exists to "act pro-environmentally." If a person knows throwing batteries in the trash is bad, they might hesitate. However, if they learn that *rechargeable* batteries are "especially toxic," they may rationalize that throwing away *regular* batteries is acceptable.
**Implication**: Concrete information can sometimes be used to "stretch" an abstract norm to favor a hedonic goal (avoiding the effort of recycling).

## Raw Text Snippet: Definition of Oughtness
"A feeling of oughtness of a social norm has at least three components. The first component is a sense of importance... Second, oughtness implies that one disapproves of others’ transgressing the norm... Third, oughtness implies that one feels obliged to follow the norm oneself." (p. 38)

===SECTION===
## Raw Text Snippet: Smart Norms
"Smart norms... need to be translated to a given situation... This has the advantage that it allows normative behavior to flexibly adapt to changing situations. Yet, there is also a large disadvantage: smart norms open the door wide for moral hypocrisy, allowing individuals to twist and stretch the translation of the abstract norm." (p. 39)
